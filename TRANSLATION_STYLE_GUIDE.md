# Montafon Moonlight Translation Style Guide

**A Korean-to-English Translation of Jeong Chanju's Novel**

---

## 1. Project Overview

*Montafon Moonlight* is a serialized Korean novel by Jeong Chanju (pen name Byeongnok), following Author Jeong and his wife Mrs. Park on their journey through Austria. The narrative weaves Buddhist philosophy, Korean cultural sensibility, and meditations on art, silence, and creativity.

The translation aims to preserve the contemplative, literary quality of the original while rendering it naturally in English. This guide establishes standards for voice, terminology, and stylistic choices.

---

## 2. Voice and Tone

### 2.1 General Principles

- **Literary but unaffected.** The prose should feel elegant without being precious or overwrought.
- **Sentences that breathe.** Vary sentence length purposefully. Allow descriptive passages to flow; let dialogue break with precision.
- **Contemplative without dragging.** The source text is meditative. Preserve this quality without making English readers feel the prose is slow.
- **Warmth over distance.** The narrator's perspective is intimate, not clinical. Maintain this closeness to the characters.

### 2.2 Sentence Structure

Korean syntax often delays the main verb until sentence-end and embeds multiple subordinate clauses. When translating:

- Restructure for natural English word order, but preserve the original's pacing where possible.
- Avoid choppy short sentences that lose the original's flowing quality.
- Use em-dashes (—) for parenthetical insertions that mirror Korean's embedded clauses.
- Break overly long Korean sentences into two English sentences only when necessary for clarity.

---

## 3. Dialogue

### 3.1 Dialogue Style

- **Natural and unhurried.** Characters speak in complete thoughts, not clipped fragments.
- **Age-appropriate register.** Author Jeong and Mrs. Park are mature adults; their speech should reflect this without sounding stiff.
- **Preserve Korean speech patterns where meaningful.** Certain indirect expressions or politeness markers may be worth retaining if they reveal character.

## 4. Korean Terms and Cultural References

### 4.1 Retention Policy

Retain Korean terms when they carry cultural weight that English equivalents would flatten. Provide context through the narrative rather than footnotes or glosses.

**Terms to Retain (with romanization)**

| Korean | Romanization | Usage |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| 청심환 | cheongsim-hwan | Traditional herbal medicine; context clarifies |
| 이불재 | Ibuljae | Author Jeong's hermitage name |
| 정 | jeong | Korean concept of deep affection/bond |
| 인연 | inyeon | Karmic connection; destined encounter |
| 화두 | hwadu | Seon meditation question |

**Terms to Translate**

Generic terms without unique cultural resonance should be translated. For example, common foods, standard honorifics, or everyday objects.

## 5. Buddhist and Philosophical Content

### 5.1 Integration Principles

- **Organic, never didactic.** Buddhist philosophy should emerge through narrative and dialogue, not exposition.
- **Preserve the lecture-within-narrative frame.** When chapters adopt a pedagogical mode (as in Chapter 16 on Buddhism in Korea), maintain this structure—it is intentional.
- **Warm, not academic.** Even technical Buddhist content should feel accessible to general readers.

## 6. Formatting Conventions

### 6.1 Punctuation

- **Em-dashes (—):** Use for parenthetical insertions and interruptions. No spaces around em-dashes.
- **Ellipses (…):** Use the single-character ellipsis, not three periods. Reserve for trailing off or significant pauses.
- **Quotation marks:** Use curly (smart) quotes, not straight quotes. Periods and commas inside; colons and semicolons outside.

### 6.2 Italics

Use italics for:
- Retained Korean terms on first occurrence (subsequent uses may be roman)
- Titles of books, films, and artworks
- Internal thoughts when distinguished from dialogue
- Emphasis (sparingly)

### 6.3 Chapter Structure

- Each chapter begins with a date stamp in italics when present (e.g., *Morning, July 28, 2025*)
- Chapters end with *To be continued*
- No chapter titles—numbered only

### 6.4 Translator's Notes

- Use sparingly, only when cultural context is essential and cannot be woven into the narrative
- Place at end of chapter in a styled box/section
- Keep brief and informative, not academic

## 8. Historical and European Content

When chapters shift to historical exposition (Napoleon, Goethe, Roman history):

- Maintain the essayistic, reflective register
- These passages connect to Author Jeong's inner contemplation
- Preserve the sense of a cultured narrator musing on parallels
- Use standard English names for historical figures (Napoleon, Beethoven, Goethe)

---

## 9. Quality Standards

### 9.1 What to Preserve

- The contemplative pacing of the original
- Cultural specificity where it adds depth
- The warmth between Author Jeong and Mrs. Park
- The interplay between silence and speech, stillness and movement
- The integration of Buddhist thought into everyday observation
- Mr. Willi's unique philosophy of music and absolute silence

### 9.2 What to Avoid

- Over-explanation of cultural references
- Flattening nuance into generic equivalents
- Choppy prose that loses the original's flow
- Overly formal or academic register
- Translationese—English that reads as if translated

### 9.3 The Test

> A successful translation should read as if it were originally written in English by a writer steeped in Korean Buddhist sensibility. The reader should feel they are entering a world, not reading a document.
