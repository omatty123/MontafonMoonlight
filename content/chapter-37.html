The next day.

Author Jeong and Mrs. Park went once more to Mr. Willi’s house. As soon as they entered, Mr. Willi sat down on the piano bench and explained to them the sound of raindrops he had heard on the Inn River in Innsbruck when he was seventeen.

“Now, the first raindrop fell with a soft tap into the flowing water of the Inn. In that moment I was able to hear something very unusual. It was a resonance, a sound at a fixed pitch. But I could not make sense of the resonance or the tone at all. Still, that resonance and sound seized me. Some time passed. Then I had the chance to play the piano. I began playing like this, trying to find that resonance and sound on the piano.”

Mr. Willi pressed the keys as he spoke.

“Like this, pressing the keys one after another with my fingers, I worked for a long time to find the resonance and sound I remembered listening by the river. Then at a certain moment, I realized it was not one note but two. It was never a single note; it was two notes with two different characters. One was lower, and the other was higher. The lower note sounded like a cello, and the slightly higher one like a clarinet. It was like the sound made when a raindrop falls into the river, the sound of it springing back up again, and the sound of the raindrop entering the water. I’ll recreate the sound of the raindrop on the piano now.”

Mr. Willi simultaneously pressed the lower note, the C key, which he said a cello could make, and the higher note, the G key an octave above it, which he said could be expressed by a clarinet. Then he said,

“The piano sound I just played is the all-inclusive sound of everything contained in the exact moment when that raindrop fell. As you said, Author Jeong, one drop of water is the universe, and the universe is one drop of water.”

“So your raindrop and my water drop are connected like that.”

“What you just heard in the piano note contains many things. I would not claim it is everything. But, for example, just as the universe holds many stars, a single note can hold many sounds.”

Mr. Willi was deeply serious.

“In any case, after several months I found on the piano a note that contained the sound of a raindrop falling into the river, the sound of it bouncing off the water, and the sound of it being absorbed by the river. I found the sound of the cello and the clarinet in the piano.”

“A cello and a clarinet? Honestly, I don’t quite understand.”

“I had found that sound in which the cello and clarinet sounds had completely ripened together and were heard as one whole. For months, there were days when I forgot even to eat. I kept searching for that sound, searching and searching again.”

“How did you feel after discovering the sound of the cello and clarinet? Didn’t you at least dance?”

“I did nothing.”

Author Jeong and Mr. Willi smiled as they spoke back and forth.

“I was satisfied simply because I had found what I was looking for.”

“After you discovered that sound, how did the world feel when you looked at it?”

“In dir etwas, something within you. That was all I felt: there was something inside me.”

“Did you not feel you wanted to compose?”

“At that time, I had no thought of wanting to become a composer. Not the slightest. I had found the sound of the raindrop, so I simply felt relieved. Since I had found what I had been searching for, I thought only, ‘Now it’s done.’ What changed in me was that a different version of seeing this world had opened.” When lunchtime came, Chairwoman Kwon moved the instant rice Mrs. Park had brought, along with kimchi, gim, and gochujang, to the dining table. Mr. Willi mixed the microwaved instant rice with kimchi and gochujang and ate it with relish. But he did not touch the gim. He did not even try a taste. It seemed he felt some resistance toward the unfamiliar dark green sheet. Chairwoman Kwon said,

“People here in Montafon probably don’t know gim. It must feel burdensome to eat something they are seeing for the first time. Still, kimchi has become globalized, so Austrians eat it well too.”

Mr. Willi pronounced “kimchi” exactly. When they had taken a commemorative photograph at the entrance of the Posthotel Taube, too, he had made Author Jeong smile by saying “kimchi.”

After lunch, the party sat around the round table in the living room and spent about an hour listening to music. Mr. Willi put CDs of his own compositions into the stereo CD player and gave brief explanations.

“The opening of the first piece is played by a female clarinetist from Israel. She is a very famous musician.”

The opening of the piece began low and slowly, with a deep tone like a temple bell. Author Jeong said what he felt as he listened.

“It sounds like someone praying.”

“It is a drum sound, but yes, it is like that.”

As the CD listening was drawing to an end, Mr. Willi came out of his bedroom carrying a crucifixion figure of Jesus. The work on the living room wall had been carved by Mr. Willi’s grandfather, and the work on the dining-room wall by his father; Author Jeong and Mrs. Park wondered who had carved this crucifixion figure he had brought out now. Mr. Willi said,

“This work was carved by my great-grandfather.”

“Your great-grandfather, grandfather, and father, three generations, all carved crucifixion figures of Jesus. That is an extraordinary family tradition.”

“They were all people of deep faith. And they must also have been very gifted with their hands.”

“Seeing that you keep images of Jesus in the living room, dining room, and bedroom, Mr. Willi, I think your faith must be deep as well.”

“My father said it was made by cutting the hardest wood in the Montafon forest. If the wood was that hard, he must have broken several knives.”

If the wood had been soft, it would surely have been spruce; if it was hard, it must have been oak or beech. Since the material was said to be hard wood, it must, as Mr. Willi said, have been difficult enough to carve that knives would break against it.

“My great-grandfather is said to have carved as many as eight crucifixion figures of Jesus. One of them had gone to a certain wealthy man, and my father bought it back by adding a considerable sum of money to pieces of furniture he had made with his own hands. That was how exceptional my great-grandfather’s carving was.”

Indeed, even to Author Jeong’s eye, the carving was astonishing. The suffering expression on Jesus’s face, the even teeth visible between his lips, and the fingernails and toenails of his nailed hands and feet were all carved with vivid clarity.

“Whenever I see the figure of Jesus crucified, the Buddha’s extreme ascetic practice overlaps with it in my mind.”

“Ah, I see.”

“Jesus was resurrected, and Siddhartha attained Buddhahood. Thinking of it that way, perhaps suffering is an energy of blessing, like love opening through tears. Mr. Willi, your life in music also began when, in the suffering of an incurable illness, you encountered that raindrop.”

At the end of Author Jeong’s words, Mr. Willi closed his eyes. His expression seemed hesitant. Or perhaps the words “incurable illness” had summoned something within him. Presently Mr. Willi said,

“If you do not want to hear the story I am about to tell, I will not tell it. It is something that truly happened to me, but it is so mysterious and religious that I am cautious.”

“How could I, for any reason or on what grounds, deny what you’ve said happened to you? I want to hear it.”

Mr. Willi began his story, sadly, as though forcing himself to summon the memory. It was six months before he was discharged from Innsbruck General Hospital after receiving the doctor’s opinion that recovery was impossible. Mr. Willi’s health was at its worst. Because he had received radiation treatment for two years to treat the cancer that had spread through his whole body, he could not even swallow a drop of water properly.

To return to Montafon, Mr. Willi boarded a train at Innsbruck station. Since he deliberately did not want to meet anyone, he got into a carriage where no passengers were boarding. Once on the train, he deliberately went to the last carriage, thinking it would be quiet. But just as the train was about to move, a middle-aged woman climbed into that last carriage. As if she were a relative, she kept talking to him. When Mr. Willi did not answer, the last carriage sank into silence. About an hour passed before the woman began preparing to get off the train. It was two or three minutes before they parted. Then Mr. Willi, without knowing why, said to her,

“You have one daughter and one son.”

Mr. Willi told her the birthdays of her daughter and son and asked whether he was right.

“You are right. But you are sick with a disease that will kill you.”

The woman wrote down a telephone number and said,

“Call this number. You will be able to receive help.”

Mr. Willi was confused as to whether the brief conversation he had had with the woman was real or unreal. If he told anyone, they might be startled or laugh at him. So he said nothing, not even to his father, and kept his mouth shut. When he came home, Mr. Willi’s father, far from despairing, showed even greater resolve.

“Willi, now let us find our own path by which you can recover. I will spare no means or method to find that path. If necessary, I will sell this house and put everything in order to restore you to health.”

So after asking here and there, the place he went with his father was a hospital in Zurich. But after all the tests were finished, the female doctor’s opinion was the same as that of the doctor at Innsbruck General Hospital. The cancer had spread from his joints to his esophagus. Since the cancer had spread throughout his body, she stated flatly that there was now no method of treatment. In that moment, stillness flowed through the hospital room. The air seemed to have turned into desolate silence. Then a voice that did not seem to be human came to him.

“Herbert, you will become healthy.”

Mr. Willi could not know the reason either, but certainty arose in him. After returning home, he tried gradually to reduce the pills he was taking. It was the only method left to him. But he had become dependent on the medication and could not live even for a moment without taking it. When the effect wore off, the joints in his arms and legs hurt enough to drive him nearly mad. Still, Mr. Willi decided to reduce the number of pills by one every day. It was an attempt to stop taking them altogether once the pain in his joints and throughout his body disappeared. His effort had an effect. There was even a day when the pain in his body disappeared though he had taken no pills at all. Unfortunately, that condition lasted only for that day.
