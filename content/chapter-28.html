Cinque Terre means "five villages." The settlements had been built like nests on steep hillsides by Christians who crossed the Mediterranean to escape religious persecution. Author Jeong and his wife decided to visit Manarola, the most beautiful of the five, and followed Mr. Guster out of their lodgings. Even in Italy, local trains showed their character just as they did in Korea. It was twenty minutes late. After feeding their tickets into a validation machine that looked like a vacuum cleaner, they boarded. The inside of the local train was noisy with chatter. Italians, it seemed, shared a temperament similar to that of Koreans. Still, the local train was reasonably fast.

Before long, the car fell quiet. The ponytailed young men in the seats ahead, their arms and necks covered in tattoos, had gotten off. The train passed through Forte dei Marmi, where Europe's largest marble quarry came into view. Marble had been quarried there since the Renaissance, so Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo must have used stone from this very place for their sculptures.

Author Jeong and his wife hurried off at La Spezia to transfer trains. A train bound for Cinque Terre was stopped at the station. Perhaps because Cinque Terre had been transformed into a tourist destination, travelers poured onto the connecting train. Mr. Guster explained kindly.

"If you get off at La Spezia station and walk, you come to the sea. There at the pier, passenger ferries run back and forth to Cinque Terre. Near the pier, there is also the 'Manzoni Walk.' Alessandro Manzoni was an Italian novelist whose work, The Betrothed, published in 1840, is regarded as comparable to Dante's Divine Comedy."

Since he had never read Manzoni's novel, Author Jeong kept quiet and simply listened. Mr. Guster went on to explain The Betrothed.

"He is a central figure in Italy's literary tradition, regarded as a master of Italian serious literature and the classics. The Betrothed is so famous that Italians often say everyone has read it. I read it myself at seventeen, but the world of the novel was so hard to grasp that it was difficult to become absorbed in it."

Mr. Guster added that as a teenager he had attended a Gymnasium in Switzerland, and that to graduate one had to read all the masterpieces of European literature. In other words, even though it was considered difficult for its time, he had read The Betrothed because he graduated from the Gymnasium. Manarola station was in the second of the five villages. The station stood on cliffs above the shore.

Author Jeong and his wife followed the kind Mr. Guster up the sloping path into the village. They wanted to see the church first. They felt that the sweat and tears of the residents who had turned these barren hillsides into terraced fields must have seeped into the church. Standing in the churchyard, they could take in the terraced fields and the sea in a single glance. The church was modest, but Schumacher's words came to mind: "Small is beautiful." The church was like a flower the settlers had made bloom with their tears. Looking up at the bronze bell hanging in the tower, they sensed a weighty vow within it, a promise not to forget the years of hardship.

On both sides of the sloping path down to the sea, black-and-white photographs of settlers carving out lives in these cliff-like mountains were on display. Author Jeong thought that these photographs were what one truly ought to see in Cinque Terre. As they climbed and descended the narrow coastal paths, they came upon a hillside where the deceased had been given wind burials. It was strikingly similar to what he had seen on Wando in Korea. It was a funerary custom created by settlers in rugged terrain where there was simply not enough land to bury the dead. The alleys ran like a maze. Climbing up through them without any plan, Jeong saw the sea once more spreading out below the cliffs. On the gate of one of the houses, he noticed a nameplate bearing a familiar name. "Noticed" was too mild: he had made a discovery. Author Jeong cried out.

"The poet Montale stayed at this house!"

Eugenio Montale was a leading Italian poet from Genoa who received the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1975. Jeong had cried out because he had once been deeply moved by Montale's poetry collection Cuttlefish Bones, published in Korean by Minumsa.

The house Montale had used as his study, however, felt abandoned and forlorn, as though no one lived there any longer. When he won the Nobel Prize, every place he had ever stayed must have buzzed with excitement. As if consoling the poet's spirit, Author Jeong murmured lines from the poem "Mediterranean," which seemed to have drawn inspiration from Cinque Terre:

Old friend, I was drunk
on the sounds that burst forth
from your lips, a blue bell
opening and closing again.
The house where I lived each passing summer
was, yes, yes, near you,
where the sun blazed
and mosquitoes formed clouds in the sky.
O sea, today as then
I grow numb before you.
How could I ever receive
the sublime counsel your breath imparts?
The faint beating of my heart
is no more than a moment of your breathing.
Your stern law
is lodged deep within my heart,
urging me to be broad, various, and firm.
As you hurl all the rubbish from the abyss
onto the shore's starfish, cork scraps, and seaweed,
so I too must wash away all impurity:
that is what you first told me.

It was something of a stretch, but Author Jeong and his wife chose Siena as their second destination. They needed to bring their Italy trip to a close and go to Vienna the next day, since Chairwoman Kwon was waiting for them there. Having heard so much from Mr. Guster about Siena's history and culture on the way to Florence, the city did not feel unfamiliar. On the ride to Siena, home to the Piazza del Campo that Mr. Willi loved, Author Jeong turned his attention to an Italian travel guide.

"Located on a hill 320 meters above sea level, roughly 50 kilometers south of Florence, between tributaries of the Ombrone River. The Chianti hills are celebrated for their wine. Papal and Spanish forces vied with each other for control of Siena. After 1559, it became part of the Duchy of Tuscany. Siena's Renaissance reached its zenith in the fourteenth century, and in the first half of that century a Sienese school of painting emerged in conscious relation to the Florentine school. Because the city was slow to develop as a modern metropolis, many old buildings remain. Landmarks include Siena Cathedral, which rivaled Florence Cathedral, the Piazza del Campo where the Palio horse race is held, and the city hall with the Torre del Mangia."

After transferring to the train bound for Siena, Author Jeong dozed off. When he woke, the train was passing through broad pastureland, with scattered groves of trees dotting the endless meadows.

"Mr. Willi must have lived in one of those forests of absolute silence, composing in his mind."

It was enviable that primeval forest management had been enshrined in law so that animals could live there. Before long the train was running through mountain country. It looked for all the world like Daegwallyeong Pass in Korea's Gangwon Province. Siena station was nestled in a gorge.

Stepping off at Siena station, the sky was as blue as the sea at Cinque Terre. Through deep green leaves, an old castle was visible in the distance, and here too, as in Cinque Terre, a church bell tower rose high. To reach the city quickly from the station, one had to take escalators. It felt like rising some ten floors at a Korean department store. It was a city formed on unusual terrain. Passing through a gate like a triumphal arch, they emerged at once into Siena's center, the historic district designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The streets were narrow, overflowing with travelers. Walking through an alley, they came upon the building where the Sienese novelist Federigo Tozzi had lived. A little later, in a small piazza, they met a street artist drawing on the paving stones. Thinking of his younger daughter who did illustration, Author Jeong pulled a coin from his pocket.

At last they stepped into the Piazza del Campo, Siena's center, said to have been laid out in the thirteenth century. The piazza was shaped like a seashell and every year since the fourteenth century it had hosted the Palio. Representatives from each district, dressed in fourteenth century traditional costume, paraded with their local banners and then competed on horseback without saddles in a race so fierce that riders falling and dying was possible. That Mr. Willi, who lived in absolute silence, liked it struck Author Jeong as more than a little puzzling. In any case, it seemed to reflect the rough temperament of the people of Siena.

After viewing the Torre del Mangia, said to have been built high like an observation tower to ward off the Black Death, Mrs. Park bought an apron from a street vendor, thinking it would be useful for her pottery work. Together they then hurried after Mr. Guster to the Santa Maria Cathedral. The cost of the tickets did not feel wasted. The entire interior of the cathedral was a treasure. They could not take their eyes off the pulpit carved with lions, the black and white marble mosaic patterns, and the biblical scenes. In a side room, the Piccolomini Museum, there was even a musical score written by Handel.

Mr. Guster explained that construction of the cathedral had begun in the twelfth century and was completed in the mid-fourteenth. It was a structure completed over the course of two hundred years. Giovanni Pisano's sculptures had become cultural symbols of their cities wherever one went, and Author Jeong could not help recalling again and again, trite as it might be, the saying: "Life is short, art is long." On the way down from the cathedral, they passed the house of Cesare Brandi, a historical critic, novelist, and poet. Born in Siena and having died there as well, he could be considered a local writer. Crossing the Piazza del Campo once more, they walked briskly to catch their train. The train was smaller than the one they had taken on the way in, with only two cars, and the 5:38 departure was again delayed.
