5:30 in the morning.

Above the mountain in front, a round waxing half-moon, bright only on its right side, hung in the sky. Its light flooded the valley. Dawn was breaking beyond the peaks, and the lights seeping from the houses along the valley slopes and in the basin looked like sparks clinging to a great lump of glowing charcoal. It was mysterious, as if those lights were answering the moonlight in the sky. At that moment, Author Jeong suddenly remembered Mr. Willi’s pure figure—the moment when he bowed to the audience, in footage he had watched in Mr. Willi’s living room from the premieres of the Vienna Philharmonic’s 150th-anniversary commemorative opera Encounter and Brother of Sleep, the opera commemorating the thousandth anniversary of the founding of Austria.

Back then, Mr. Willi had been an unspoiled country youth from Montafon. Montafon was a remote borderland backcountry village, as much as six hundred kilometers west of Austria’s capital, Vienna. Cross just one mountain and you were on Swiss soil; go a little south and you were in Italy.

The Mr. Willi in the video was pure and innocent. No matter how carefully that silver-haired country youth had dressed himself, it seemed the aristocrats of Vienna and self-proclaimed intellectuals would have whispered among themselves on seeing him, “That poor country bumpkin has made it!” The loose suit that looked borrowed, the small frame, the quick little stride that seemed almost to run, the comic sight of him bowing deeply like a child with both hands on his belly, all this called to mind the British comedian Charlie Chaplin. And yet his blue eyes, now that he was nearly seventy, remained as clear as the jade-green waters of a Montafon valley stream.

As they had the day before, Chairwoman Kwon, Author Jeong, and Mrs. Park began their day at Mr. Willi’s house. Author Jeong asked the questions, Mr. Willi answered, and Chairwoman Kwon interpreted. Mrs. Park took careful notes. At one moment, Author Jeong and Mr. Willi understood each other even without interpretation. It turned out that Chairwoman Kwon, instead of interpreting into German, had kept speaking Korean to Mr. Willi. When Author Jeong pointed out to her that she was speaking Korean without interpreting, she laughed out loud. Mr. Willi and Mrs. Park burst into laughter along with her.

They spoke about the Max Weiler painting they had seen the day before in Christoph’s office.

“Max Weiler’s abstract-style painting is still lingering before my eyes.”

“He is one of the modern painters representative of Austria. Weiler’s painting is a familiar work, one I had seen even when I was suffering from an incurable illness.”

With eyes that seemed sunk in memory, Mr. Willi spoke of his inyeon with Max Weiler’s painting. In Montafon, he had a friend named Jerry, with whom he had played in his first band. Jerry was a multi-talented friend who could play any instrument at all. Even while hiking, if they entered a bar with a performance stage, he would play at once if someone asked him, no matter what instrument was there, piano, violin, clarinet, whatever.

That friend’s uncle owned an apartment in Innsbruck. While Mr. Willi was ill, the uncle gave him permission to stay there for two months. One Max Weiler painting hung on the wall beside the bed in the room where Willi slept.

“When I lay in bed, the painting was by my head, so I had no choice but to see it often. However, after I came home, the pain of illness made me simply forget it. Still, that unfamiliar Weiler painting left a deep impression on me. It was my first encounter with abstract art.”

“When I was in high school, I was so captivated by Van Gogh that, when I painted in the art club, I often tried to imitate him. Ha ha ha.”

Mr. Willi met Max Weiler in person in June 1999. His friend Franz Endler had invited him to Bregenz, and they were traveling together. A few days later, Willi visited Weiler in Krems. At the time, Weiler was ninety, and Willi received from him an art book signed in his own hand. The aged painter passed away two years later, in 2001. Even after Weiler’s death, Willi stayed in phone contact for some time with his wife, Yvonne Weiler.

Only then did Mr. Willi fall deeply under the spell of Weiler’s paintings. What moved him especially was Weiler’s artistic philosophy, which had consistently pursued abstraction. When Weiler first made his name as a painter in 1941, he mainly painted figurative works, but from 1958 onward he pursued abstraction. Mr. Willi even attempted to incorporate Weiler’s style into his own composition.

“Looking at Weiler’s painting style, which kept reducing and simplifying the painterly means of points, lines, and planes, I wondered what such reduction or simplification might be in music. If you take more and more out of music, what essence remains? In the end I concluded that perhaps only a flowing melody and a rhythm pulsing like a heartbeat moving at regular intervals would remain. I realized that in music there is a limit to becoming abstract. Reduce it any further, and it is no longer music. Though in painting, of course, empty space can become part of the work, as in East Asian painting.”

“I remember your friend Christoph saying he loved and respected Weiler. Perhaps he found some intersection between your music and Weiler’s paintings. I think Christoph likes artists who strive to embody essence rather than phenomenon, whether in art or in music.”

“After seeing Weiler’s painting in my friend’s uncle’s apartment in Innsbruck, so many years passed, and then I encountered another Weiler painting in Christoph’s office. I can only marvel at this long, invisible inyeon.”

“I often tell my acquaintances that the arrow of inyeon finds its target even in the night.”

Mr. Willi struck the pose of shooting an arrow, as if to show he agreed. As had been the case every time, the time Author Jeong and Mr. Willi spent exchanging words passed very quickly. It was never dull or tedious at all. Looking at the wall clock, they saw that more than two hours had already gone by. Mr. Willi made a proposal.

“If we ride the cable car up, we will come to a quiet hillside. I secluded myself for a year in a log house there, too, while composing. Shall we go there now? The mayor of Silbertal in Montafon is a friend who likes my music.”

They traveled in Mr. Willi’s car. The Silbertal town office was smaller and more modest than a Korean village office. After getting out of the car, Mr. Willi said he would go inside the office and introduce them to the mayor. But before they could enter, the mayor was already standing on the steps in front of the building. The appearance of Erwin Heizle, mayor of Silbertal in Montafon, was the complete opposite of the big-city mayor of Bregenz whom they had met the day before. Wearing work clothes, he had been talking with a staff member when he greeted Mr. Willi. Mr. Willi introduced Author Jeong. The mayor then asked whether the place where Author Jeong lived was a tourist destination in Korea. When Author Jeong said that Hwasun-gun in Jeollanam-do also had many tourist sites, the mayor said:

“It would be good for Montafon and your region to conclude a tourism MOU.”

“I know the county governor where I live quite well, so when I go to Korea, I will suggest it.”

The mayor seemed busy; rather than having tea inside the office, he walked ahead and said they should go straight to the cable car. He bought the party’s tickets himself at the ticket window, then guided them to the waiting cable car. As soon as they boarded, he began explaining Silbertal like a Korean tourism commentator.

“Silbertal means ‘valley of silver.’ It is a village with a history of flourishing in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries as a mining area for silver and copper. Among our village’s attractions are the Montafon Mining Museum and the small St. Agatha Miners’ Chapel, where miners prayed for safety. And the Neue Heilbronner Hütte and Wormser Hütte, located at 2,320 meters above sea level, are famous hiking destinations where visitors can fully enjoy the pure atmosphere of the Alps and taste local traditional cuisine.”

It was an introduction that showed how much the mayor loved Silbertal. As soon as they got off the cable car, he led the party to a restaurant. On the way, Mr. Willi pointed to a small log cabin and said:

“Leaving the bustling village down in the valley below, I secluded myself for a year in this very log house. Living there, I again encountered absolute stillness.”

The mayor paid for lunch and then returned at once. After the meal, the party also looked out over patches of well-tended pasture, drank a cup of coffee, and rode the cable car back down. It was not far from Silbertal to Mr. Willi’s house. The party went straight up to the dining room with Mr. Willi’s round table. Then Author Jeong proposed a name he had come up with for Mr. Willi’s studio-home.

“It just occurred to me. How would it be to call your house ‘Montafon Moonlight House’?”

“That would be very good.”

Mr. Willi accepted it gladly.

“My studio is called Ibuljae 耳佛齋. It is written in Chinese characters: ear i (耳), Buddha bul (佛), and house jae (齋). It means a house where one hears the sounds of water and the wind and attains Buddhahood, truth. People from many walks of life come to Ibuljae, receive comfort, gain courage, and leave having been healed, so I think the studio lives up to its name.”

At this, Mr. Willi suddenly wore a lonely expression and said:

“I am poor, and my house is small. Could I really welcome many people? And someone may come using my name to contrive some scheme.”

For the first time, Author Jeong dimly sensed that Mr. Willi was a composer living in straitened circumstances. He was a composer representative of Europe and Austria, yet behind his stature and fame, poverty lay in wait as a hard reality. Is not an artist a being like daybreak, calling forth the morning? And if poverty torments an artist, is there no way to resolve it? Author Jeong suddenly felt gloomy, too. In that moment, the thought surged up in him that if the novel he had written taking Mr. Willi as its subject succeeded, he wanted to help Mr. Willi first.

“If Montafon Moonlight succeeds, more than anything, I would like to help you first, Mr. Willi.”

At Author Jeong’s single remark, the mood in the living room turned solemn in an instant. Chairwoman Kwon, who had acted like Mr. Willi’s older sister, finally burst into audible sobs, as if reproaching herself for cherishing him yet being unable to become any great source of strength. Then moisture welled up in Mr. Willi’s large eyes. After a moment, Mr. Willi waved his hands, telling Author Jeong he did not need to help him, but Author Jeong promised that because the words had already left his mouth, he would certainly honor them. Author Jeong said:

“The roughly seven thousand pages of scores stored in your underground studio, Mr. Willi, will one day rise higher in renown than Mont Blanc.”

At Author Jeong’s words of comfort, the dining room regained a little of its liveliness. Author Jeong spoke again.

“Because you, Mr. Willi, are a being like the moon of Montafon, one day you will brightly illuminate the world, and the people of the world, too, will not forget their gratitude.”

In that moment, not only Mr. Willi but even Chairwoman Kwon and Mrs. Park’s faces brightened again like the moonlight of Montafon.
