When I drew back the curtains, light came pouring in. Suddenly I thought of Korea, the land where the sun rises. Perhaps the thought came to me because today we would spend one more night in Salzburg, then go to Vienna tomorrow for the day’s appointments before returning home on a night flight.

Salzburg was a city known as the birthplace of Mozart, a sacred ground of music. Among the stories Mr. Willi had told them in Montafon, the story of an important nobleman in the music world, known as Karajan’s right-hand man, naturally came to mind. That nobleman, who possessed one of Mozart’s unfinished scores, had sent Mr. Willi a piece of mail bearing his seal. It had arrived when Mr. Willi was pressed for time, composing the requested work Ego eimi for performance during the Salzburg Festival.

For that reason, Mr. Willi could not open the mail right away. A few days later, he opened it and found, along with the nobleman's letter, Mozart's unfinished score. The letter asked him to complete the unfinished score. The Mozart score he had sent, about three minutes in performance length, was an unfinished score with neither beginning nor end. In his letter, the nobleman said he had entrusted Mozart's unfinished score to Mr. Willi because he had seen the record of Mr. Willi's life described in detail in an opera, and because the sums of the digits in Mozart's and Mr. Willi's birthdates were the same. Mozart's birthday was January 27, 1756, and Willi's was January 7, 1956, so the digit sums of both birthdates were both 29. The nobleman called himself a researcher of numbers.

Mr. Willi wondered whether he should grant the request.

“Of all Austria’s many composers, it was an honor, and something I was grateful for, that he asked me. But because I was composing Ego eimi, it was not easy to agree at once.”

Mr. Willi set it aside for another week. Then, at a certain moment, he had a presentiment that the composition might come of its own accord.

"At that time, in the fact that the digit sums of Mozart's and my birthdates were the same, I felt a fateful inspiration. In that instant, I thought, 'Perhaps it is all right for me to complete Mozart's unfinished score.' A kind of confidence or inspiration rose up in me. So I called him and told him, 'I will do it.'"

Still, Mr. Willi attached one condition for the nobleman.

“Two hundred years from now, I hope some Austrian composer will complete Mozart’s unfinished score in yet another way. Austrians have no choice but to love Mozart forever.”

And so, for the first movement, Mr. Willi took inspiration from the fact that the digit sums of the two birthdates were both 29; it was not easy, but he made music using numbers. For the second movement, he inserted Mozart's unfinished score exactly as it was. For Willi, no alteration whatsoever was possible in the second movement Mozart had composed, and there was no way he could add his own music to it. In a word, there was not even one percent of room for Willi's music to intrude upon Mozart's. The third movement portrayed human beings in the realities of life, and the fourth movement concluded in Mr. Willi's own manner of composition: music from which the concept of time had departed, beginning in absolute silence.

"This is only my thought, but the identical number 29, the digit sum of both Mozart's and Mr. Willi's birthdates, feels like a secret code symbolizing Mozart's reincarnation, his resurrection. The nobleman who noticed this fateful number and approached Mr. Willi even seems to me like an astrologer draped in a black cloak."

"In any case, when I gave the completed four-movement work its title, I put the numbers into it. The title is Kairos im Kronos 1756 / 1956, and it was premiered by the Vienna Philharmonic. The principal player said, 'It felt as if we had played a Mozart piece.' Not only that, he also said that in the third and fourth movements he had discovered a commonality between Mozart's music and Willi's music."

“What was the commonality the principal player felt?”

It was one of Author Jeong’s keen questions, asked in Mr. Willi’s dining room while they were staying in Montafon. Mr. Willi answered cautiously but kindly.

“The age of Habsburg Empress Maria Theresa, when court music and dance music prevailed, and the modern age, when even jazz has appeared, have different historical backgrounds. So I cannot speak rashly. But I suppose that Mozart and I may share at least this: that we both saw music at the boundary between the visible and the invisible.”

Mr. Willi, now as then, always said he “saw” music; he did not say he “heard” it.

The television in the lodging’s living room showed that the festival streets of Salzburg were a sea of people. It reported that every hotel in the city center was full of tourists gathered to enjoy the festival. Silvia, with sunglasses perched on her head, gave her morning greeting at the outdoor table.

“Guten Morgen!”

“Good morning.”

Chairwoman Kwon, too, greeted Author Jeong and Mrs. Park, translating as she did so.

“This morning, we are going to Hohensalzburg Fortress, built into the rocky mountain. It is the fortress Mr. Willi told us we must visit.”

“The concert is in the evening, so I would like to see the fortress at leisure.”

“The fortress has an excellent view, including a view of all of Salzburg. So take some commemorative photographs, sit on a bench and rest, and let’s return to the hotel around four.”

After breakfast, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park, together with Chairwoman Kwon, rode in the passenger car Silvia was driving and entered a street close to Hohensalzburg Fortress. Silvia drove skillfully and found a relatively quiet parking lot. They would have to walk through one alley and then another to the fortress entrance, but that did not matter. Silvia’s dog suddenly relieved itself, but Silvia cleaned it up neatly with a black plastic bag.

At last, when the party climbed up to the fortress, they sat on a bench and looked down over the city of Salzburg. The Salzach River flowed through the center of the city, and Hohensalzburg Fortress rose midway along the city’s north-south axis. Whenever she had a chance, Silvia, like the professor she was, unpacked what she knew.

“When summer came, the Habsburg rulers would come from Vienna to Salzburg and spend the season here. Because there was an imperial residence, the city carries a sense of refinement and dignity, whether one notices it or not. Two hundred fifty million years ago, Salzburg and the Alpine region were the floor of the sea. As periods when the seawater dried and evaporated repeated, enormous amounts of salt settled and formed sedimentary strata. That is why Salzburg has salt mines. Beginning one hundred million years ago, the African continental plate moved north and collided forcefully with the European continental plate, causing Salzburg and the Alps, once beneath the sea, to surge upward.”

“Salzburg, I notice, is pronounced something like Jjalzburg. But salt, after all, is jjada, salty, in Korean, is it not? Since both jjada and Salz seem to begin with that same tense jj sound, one cannot help but sense a certain resemblance to Korean as well.”

Chairwoman Kwon said,

“You are a writer, after all. I have never once thought of jjada and ‘Salz’ together.”

“There is one other thing in Austria that made me feel a similarity between Korean and the language here. Shall I tell you? In Austria, a street is called a Gasse. In Korea, when we say, ‘Let us go somewhere,’ we say gase. So I see a similarity there.”

“My address is on Beethoven Gasse. It means the street where Beethoven passed away. Your powers of observation are remarkable, Author Jeong.”

“Shall I say I sense it instinctively, without trying? In India, cities or villages with a fortress are called pur. Jaipur, Jodhpur, Udaipur, and so on. In Austria’s Salzburg, too, Burg seems linguistically similar to pur, in the sense of a city with a fortress.”

Though India and Austria were geographically thousands of kilometers apart, it was also true that, in linguistic etymology and historical function, they displayed a surprisingly perfect parallel. Author Jeong had spoken only of what he himself had discovered, but if one looked carefully, there seemed likely to be many such similar words.

Unlike Montafon, Salzburg’s cathedrals were enormous in scale. The great cathedral in the city center, where the square lay, must have been where a cardinal resided. Mrs. Park, a classical-music devotee, asked Silvia,

“They say Mozart played the piano before Empress Maria Theresa when he was four. What was it that nurtured the musicality of Mozart, that child prodigy?”

When Silvia only smiled, Author Jeong said,

“Dear, perhaps it was the Salzach River flowing through the city and Hohensalzburg Fortress?”

“That is a vague answer, not like you.”

“Mozart must have loved the Salzach River, flowing unchanged then and now beyond time and space, more than anyone. Hohensalzburg Fortress too… Is there any material that nurtures sensibility more than love?”

Author Jeong had answered in an explanatory way, but in truth he was dissatisfied. It was because the answer felt closer to a supplementary explanation than to a clear answer. Like a hwadu, Author Jeong murmured to himself,

'What was it that nurtured Mozart's musicality?'

The party walked along the shade of the fortress wall. Every stone in the wall was sedimentary rock, bearing the dark green and deep gray of the deep seabed. Since Silvia had explained that Salzburg had once lain beneath the sea, the fortress wall itself seemed to be proving her words.

“A moment ago you told us Salzburg had been beneath the sea. It seems you were exactly right.”

“It is not only the fortress wall. Most of the walls of the cathedral in the city center are also made of stones cut from sedimentary rock.”

Suddenly Author Jeong let his imagination take wing. He imagined why Mozart had climbed up to the fortress like a man possessed, day and night, to conceive musical ideas. Perhaps Mozart, touching the sedimentary rock of the fortress wall that held the ancient sea, walking over the massive sedimentary bedrock inside the fortress, had seen the music living within it. So he guessed. Only then did Author Jeong feel the point of intersection between Mozart and Willi.

'Just as Willi saw absolute silence in the ancient primeval forest, Mozart may have seen absolute silence in primeval sedimentary rock. If so, was not absolute silence the place where the music of both composers began?'

Yet Author Jeong could not assert this as fact. That was because he had heard there were no sentences or passages about absolute silence in the records or studies concerning Mozart. Mr. Willi had told him that Mozart, too, had never used the term absolute silence.

The party returned to the hotel at four in the afternoon. They needed a proper rest, because the concert was at eight that evening. While they rested at the hotel’s outdoor table, talking with Silvia, another burst of laughter broke out. It happened when Silvia introduced her husband.

“You will meet him tomorrow; my husband is 197 centimeters tall, and his name is Boris.”

“What? Did you say borisu (Bodhi tree)?”

“Hahaha.”

Everyone burst into laughter because Author Jeong had said “Boris” as borisu, “Bodhi tree.” Perhaps embarrassed, Author Jeong explained the Bodhi tree.

“Because the Buddha attained enlightenment beneath the Bodhi tree, the Bodhi tree is a sacred tree. My wife and I personally paid our respects to that tree in Bodh Gaya, India. Its leaves are heart-shaped, and unusually, they say the Bodhi tree releases oxygen twenty-four hours a day. That must be why practitioners preferred to sit beneath a Bodhi tree breathing out sacred energy.”

“Hahaha. A Bodhi tree is growing well in our garden.”

Chairwoman Kwon added that there really was a Bodhi tree in Silvia’s garden. Chairwoman Kwon praised Silvia’s tall husband Boris as a very kind man. Once, when her car broke down in the street, she called Boris, who knew how to repair cars; although it was nighttime, he came at once, arranged for Chairwoman Kwon to go home first in his own car, then repaired her car there in the street and returned late at night.
