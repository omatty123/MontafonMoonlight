It was the day they were leaving Montafon. Author Jeong and Mrs. Park and Chairwoman Kwon went to Mr. Willi’s house by nine in the morning. Chairwoman Kwon’s friend Silvia was scheduled to come with her car by two in the afternoon, so until then they could talk with Mr. Willi. In the living room, Mr. Willi had the large monitor turned on and showed the party a video of the mountainside, about three thousand meters above sea level, that had inspired Ego eimi, where the resonance of being, music, lived within nature’s absolute silence.

“Even now, music is alive in the absolute silence of that mountainside, but as hikers’ footsteps become more frequent, the music may hide itself somewhere else. The local government says it will build a bicycle road there, and I am worried.”

Mr. Willi went over beside the large monitor, crouched down, and pointed with his finger to the mountainside where the music of Ego eimi lived. To be clear, he was not vaguely pointing toward the Alps; he was precisely indicating and explaining the exact spot where the music of Ego eimi lived.

Afterward, Mr. Willi led the party down to the basement. In the basement were the scores for Brother of Sleep, which Mr. Willi had written himself by hand. The scores were stacked neatly inside a long, narrow cabinet, piled nearly as high as Mr. Willi himself. Mr. Willi showed them one sheet of music, and the black notes looked like a swarm of ants. To Author Jeong, who had painted in the art club until high school, the scores looked like hyperrealist paintings.

“They look like hyperrealist paintings.”

“I hear composers these days work comfortably on computers. But I still work on everything by hand with a pen.”

“If facsimile copies of the scores were placed in frames and the music for those sections included on a CD, wouldn’t they become avant-garde art, and perhaps an elegant product as well?”

“I’m not sure.”

“If we discussed it with your businessman friend Christoph, I think a better idea might come to mind.”

But Mr. Willi was hesitant about commercializing his scores. He seemed to listen with interest, then soon changed the subject.

“I would like to visit the place where you live, Author Jeong.”

“Please come. We have a guest room where visitors stay. If you come, Mr. Willi, I will put a bed in there for you.”

“No, no need to do that.”

Chairwoman Kwon said,

“Mr. Willi does not show his scores to guests except on special occasions. I think he is showing them today as a gift from his heart to Author Jeong and Mrs. Park, since you are leaving Montafon this afternoon.”

“I can feel Mr. Willi’s warm heart. I, too, would like to express mine to him.”

Author Jeong gave Mr. Willi the prayer beads he had been wearing around his wrist. It was a gift of the beads he had long worn together with his watch.

“This is my heart.”

Mr. Willi, suddenly presented with the prayer beads, was delighted.

“On which wrist should I wear them?”

“Either wrist is fine. Wear them however feels comfortable.”

Mr. Willi put the prayer beads on his left wrist. His wrist was slender, almost delicate. The beads slipped out from the end of his gray jumper sleeve and hung loosely on his wrist. Then Mrs. Park, too, readily gave Chairwoman Kwon the prayer beads she had cherished. Chairwoman Kwon, who had worked so hard interpreting for them all this time, smiled brightly and said,

“It seems I have received far too much in interpreting fees.”

“Not at all. Compared with what you have given us, Chairwoman Kwon, it is far too small.”

Before leaving Mr. Willi’s house, Author Jeong once again spoke of Jeong. It had suddenly occurred to him what meaning Jeong might hold in a world stained by war. After the Second World War, peace had seemed to come briefly, yet in places across the earth, human dignity was being brutally destroyed by the madness of wars started by a few greedy leaders. Author Jeong said,

“You said the theme of the tenth movement of Jeong is absolute freedom and absolute peace, happiness and joy in which anything can be fulfilled. I think Jeong, in this age of greed stained by war, is precisely a musical answer offered to twenty-first-century humanity longing for freedom and peace, happiness and joy.”

Mr. Willi showed no reaction, as though Author Jeong had said something rather out of the blue. Author Jeong asked Chairwoman Kwon,

“Has an Austrian musician ever received the Nobel Peace Prize?”

“As far as I know, I do not think so.”

“Why not? The message of Jeong is clear. The message of Jeong is different from the claims of politicians or human-rights activists. It is music that awakens human dignity through feeling. I believe music is one of the things that appeals most powerfully to humanity longing for freedom and peace.”

Mrs. Park said,

“It seems we need the ideas of Christoph, the businessman. Because he is a businessman, I suspect he may find a better way than we can to appeal to Austrian intellectuals about the value of Mr. Willi’s Jeong.”

Only then did Chairwoman Kwon show the skill of the businesswoman who had built the IMK company, rather than simply that of an interpreter.

“If Christoph were to take the lead and organize Austrian politicians, musicians, and intellectuals who love Mr. Willi’s music, he could promote the value of Jeong effectively.”

Mr. Willi still looked as if the idea had come from nowhere. Because Author Jeong had suddenly brought up the Nobel Peace Prize, he even covered his face with both hands, as if embarrassed. But Author Jeong had only spoken sincerely.

“Jeong is a musical alternative for freedom and peace offered to the people of the world. How sacred a thing that is. I hope Montafon Moonlight becomes a stepping-stone that lets people know Mr. Willi’s philosophy of music.”

Only then did Mr. Willi gather both hands over his abdomen and show Author Jeong respect and reverence. But at that moment, Author Jeong saw a lonely shadow flicker across Mr. Willi’s face. As the time when they had to part drew nearer, the shadow grew darker. Author Jeong murmured to himself,

“Ah, once all of us leave Montafon, Mr. Willi will become lonely again. Aren’t artists by nature lonely beings?”

Chairwoman Kwon spoke with someone on her cell phone, then said,

“My friend Silvia says she is outside. We will take Silvia’s car and go to Vienna by way of Salzburg.”

When they came down the wooden stairs from the second floor of Mr. Willi’s house, five or six grannies were passing by. The sky over Montafon was covered with pale clouds. One of the grandmothers spoke to Author Jeong, whose clothing looked unfamiliar.

“Please don’t take Montafon’s sun with you.”

“Yes, I will take only Montafon’s clouds.”

At the grandmother’s wish for clear weather, Author Jeong replied that he would take “Montafon’s clouds,” and Mr. Willi, Chairwoman Kwon, and Mrs. Park all laughed. Still, Mr. Willi looked lonely and forlorn.

Silvia was Chairwoman Kwon’s friend, albeit about ten years younger. Solidly built and laughing easily, she had brought her dog with her. She often laughed loudly as she drove. Chairwoman Kwon introduced her. Silvia had lectured at a university, held doctorates in philosophy and education, and had even earned an airplane pilot’s license. Chairwoman Kwon said that a Korean high school student studying in Austria had once stayed at Silvia’s house. Then she brought up an old story: the student had nearly been expelled from school, but Silvia and Chairwoman Kwon had met with the principal and resolved the matter.

It was an incident that began with an Austrian high school teacher misunderstanding a Korean exchange student. It was a cultural difference between Korea and Austria. Whenever the Austrian teacher pointed something out, the Korean student lowered his head. Chairwoman Kwon said with a laugh,

“When an adult scolds us, we lower our heads, don’t we? But Austrian adults understand it as defiance if the other person lowers their head. You have to lift your head and look at the adult for them to think you are being obedient.”

“It is the exact opposite of us. Hahaha.”

“At that time, it was serious for us. Silvia and I went to the school. We met with the principal and I persuaded him. I told him, ‘When I was scolded by my father as a child, I lowered my head and showed that I was reflecting.’ So I pleaded with him to forgive the student, because the student lowering his head meant he was showing deference by self-reflecting, not in any way rebelling.”

Silvia also said,

“The principal understood Sonja’s words and persuaded the other teachers, so the Korean student who had been staying with me graduated from school with good grades and went on to university with the teachers’ recommendations. Now he has become a professor.”

She then proudly said that she still kept the Korean hanbok the student had given her as if it were a treasure.

The three-room lodging Silvia had reserved online stood in the countryside on the outskirts of Salzburg. Because of Silvia’s dog, Chairwoman Kwon had booked a separate room. When they arrived at the lodging, it was six in the evening. That meant they had driven four hours from Montafon to their destination. Silvia moved about energetically, as if she were not at all tired. Their lodging’s name was romantic.

Romantik Spa Hotel.

The reason Silvia had reserved a hotel on the outskirts was the difficulty of parking in downtown Salzburg. She asked Author Jeong and Mrs. Park for their understanding, explaining that during the Salzburg Festival it was extremely difficult to find parking in the city center and that she had therefore chosen accommodations on the outskirts. However, after hearing Silvia explain the hotel’s history, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park, along with Chairwoman Kwon, reassured her that it had actually been an excellent choice.

Since the hotel’s history dated back to 1334, it was an astonishing 691 years old. Since first opening its doors, it had been operated continuously by the same family for twenty-three generations, making it the oldest hotel in Austria still run by a single family. Originally an inn operated by an organic farm, it had gradually evolved over the centuries into the small yet romantic four-star hotel it is today. Chairwoman Kwon also asked Author Jeong and Mrs. Park for their understanding.

“Tomorrow evening, we are scheduled to attend a music performance, one program of the Salzburg Festival, but I was able to reserve only two tickets online. It is such a popular performance that the tickets sold out early. Only you and Mrs. Park should attend, Author Jeong.”

“Thank you, but I feel bad.”

“Not at all. Silvia and I will stay outside. We often attend concerts in Vienna.”

Silvia waved her hand, saying she could take her dog for a walk during that time. In any case, Salzburg was the city where Willi had studied composition and given himself to contemplation. Beginning in 1983, Willi studied composition under the Austrian composer Professor Helmut Eder at the Mozarteum University Salzburg.
