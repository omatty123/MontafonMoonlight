After returning from Montafon to Vienna, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park went to the framing shop located by the roadside at the entrance to Beethovengasse. They had visited the shop before departing for Montafon as well, only to find a sign on the door reading “On Vacation.” They had brought a painting from Korea and were looking for a suitable frame. The painting was one of the illustrations from Montafon Moonlight, which was already being serialized in Korea’s online Buddhist newspaper Media Buddha. It was the work of illustrator Jeong Yungyeong, selected by the artist herself. Rendered in an extremely simplified style, it depicted Chairwoman Kwon visiting Haein-sa with Mr. Willi and his companions to pay their respects.

But the sign reading “On Vacation” was still hanging on the shop door. How long the owner intended to remain away was anyone’s guess, but Author Jeong no longer had the luxury of waiting. He was due to leave Vienna the night after next. There was, however, one expedient solution: give the painting to Chairwoman Kwon now and have it framed later. When Chairwoman Kwon came to their lodging, Author Jeong said,

“Chairwoman Kwon, my daughter asked me to present this painting to you already framed, but since the owner of the framing shop is away on vacation, there seems to be no other way.”

“I know the owner well. He has spent his entire life running that shop, regarding it as his calling. When he returns from vacation, I will have the painting framed there.”

“If he has been running that shop in the same place all his life, then I would be pleased to have someone of his experience do the framing. Let me give you the painting now.”

“What a charming painting. Please convey my thanks to your daughter.”

When they came to Austria in September 2015, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park were accompanied by their daughter, Jeong Yungyeong. Having studied illustration at the graduate level at Kingston University in England, she had come along both to serve as a guide for her parents and to do some sightseeing herself. During that visit, she and Chairwoman Kwon had developed a mutual fondness for one another. In any event, Author Jeong felt genuinely relieved that the matter of the frame had been resolved, even in this fashion. There were no doubt other framing shops in central Vienna, but he found the owner known to Chairwoman Kwon more trustworthy, having devoted his entire life to the trade as a calling.

The next day.

Author Jeong and Mrs. Park took a walk from their lodging to the birthplace of Franz Schubert. The house was still closed. Perhaps, they thought, it was open only from Friday through Sunday. When they returned to their lodging, Mr. Choi, an employee of Chairwoman Kwon’s company, IMK, was waiting to guide them around Vienna for the morning. This, too, was a thoughtful gesture on Chairwoman Kwon’s part. As they walked along the street after leaving the lodging, Author Jeong asked the younger Mr. Choi, who felt almost like a son to him,

“What brought you all the way to Austria?”

Mr. Choi answered honestly.

“I majored in trumpet at university, but I gave up the demanding life of a performer and am now learning the work of concert management.”

“Does concert planning suit you?”

“I’m not sure yet. IMK presents classical concerts in a number of European cities. I travel with the team and learn as I go. My job is to arrive ahead of time in places like Paris or Prague and make the necessary preparations for the performances.”

“It sounds like a good opportunity to experience unfamiliar cultures as well.”

“You seem to travel quite a bit yourself, Author Jeong.”

“I suppose I end up traveling abroad about once a year, and whenever I do, I invariably serialize a travelogue in a newspaper or magazine. Next month, I’m planning to visit Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, and Uzbekistan.”

“I’ve been to Kazakhstan once myself, back when I was in the military.”

“What took you there?”

“I was a trumpeter in a military band. When the remains of General Hong Beomdo, who had been buried in Kazakhstan, were repatriated to Korea, I traveled to Astana as part of the delegation.”

“Are you satisfied with life here, Mr. Choi?”

“No. I’d like to return to Korea. A female employee from IMK’s Seoul office has recently come over. Once she has settled in here, I expect to return to Seoul.”

Like the young man he was, Mr. Choi spoke candidly, concealing nothing of what was on his mind. IMK undoubtedly hoped that young employees would master the work of concert management and eventually become pillars of the company, but Mr. Choi seemed to be quietly enduring the loneliness of life in a foreign land. Mrs. Park stopped at a souvenir shop and selected gifts for the members of her ceramics workshop back home.

“Something compact and lightweight, like eco bags, would be ideal.”

Mrs. Park purchased five eco bags printed with Van Gogh’s The Starry Night. She briefly considered buying bags featuring Klimt’s The Kiss as well, but decided against it, thinking such a design might not appeal to everyone. Author Jeong bought three stylish leather wallets and, in a shop beside the cathedral, selected an emerald-colored portable crucifix for his younger brother, a Catholic. They then returned to their lodging and took a short nap. Having accepted an invitation to dinner from IMK President Kwon, they felt it wise to recover a bit of their energy beforehand. President Kwon, Chairwoman Kwon’s younger brother, appeared to have inherited the business and was expanding its reach through initiatives such as signing MOUs with leading music conservatories in Europe and the United States. The task of recruiting distinguished musicians such as Mr. Willi, however, was said to remain very much Chairwoman Kwon’s domain.

The restaurant to which they had been invited was a Korean establishment. Its name, Babida, struck Author Jeong as pleasantly familiar. It was almost certainly a rendering of the Korean phrase “Bap-ida” (“It’s a meal”). President Kwon ordered bulgogi, kimchi-jjigae, and pajeon. As the meal began, he spoke optimistically about the future.

“After more than forty years of doing business in Europe, it appears that we may finally enter the American market. Europe has traditionally been driven more by music education than by the commercial side of the industry, but now our company is finally preparing to establish a presence in the United States. If we succeed there and the company takes another leap forward, we will be able to provide even greater support for Korea’s young musical talent.”

President Kwon possessed a decidedly entrepreneurial temperament. If Chairwoman Kwon had established IMK’s public-spirited identity, President Kwon seemed determined to build upon that foundation and lead the company to even greater growth. Author Jeong responded with words of encouragement and good wishes.

“I hope IMK becomes a leading force in K-Classical music.”

With K-pop, K-drama, K-film, K-food, K-beauty, K-fashion, and even K-defense industries now widely recognized, it seemed only natural that there might also be such a thing as K-Classical music. Just then, the owner of Babida, a friend of President Kwon, came over, and President Kwon introduced him.

“He originally came abroad to study flute, but gave it up, opened a Korean restaurant, and has done quite well for himself.”

“I am the novelist Jeong Chanju.”

Author Jeong handed the proprietor his business card. The moment he read it, the man looked genuinely surprised and delighted.

“I read your novel No Time Left. Isn’t Venerable Subul a kind of sage?”

“These days, rather than calling someone a sage, we would say a ‘person of the Way,’ an ‘awakened one.’”

“When I lived in Korea, I went to temples a few times. But this is the first time I’ve met an awakened person in a novel.”

The owner of Babida went so far as to sit down beside Author Jeong and, in his Gyeongsang-do accent, ask one curious question after another about the novel. Then he called his wife over and introduced Author Jeong to her as well.

“This is the author of No Time Left, the book you read too. How amazing to meet him here!”

Author Jeong was equally pleased. Meeting a reader in Vienna, the capital of Austria, was an unexpected delight. During the meal, a group of a dozen or so Korean tourists rushed in, quickly finished their bibimbap, and just as quickly departed. The owner explained that they were hurrying off to secure good viewing spots for the evening city lights, which were the next stop on their itinerary. When the meal was over, President Kwon said,

“It’s not far from here. Let me take you to a quiet country café.”

The café stood amid vineyards spread across the gentle slopes on the outskirts of Vienna. From there, one could look out over both the quietly flowing Danube and the city of Vienna below. Before taking a sip of her coffee, Chairwoman Kwon said,

“I hope your trip to Austria has been a satisfying one. If there were any shortcomings, they were due to my own inadequacies in preparing for your visit. I would be grateful for your understanding.”

“Not at all, Chairwoman Kwon. For me, the opportunity to speak with Mr. Willi each day in Montafon was a genuine joy. I believe it will become a meaningful novel.”

“I hope Montafon Moonlight will one day be published in Korean, German, and English editions.”

“When you come to Korea, we will take good care of you. If Mr. Willi comes and stays overnight at Songgwang-sa and listens closely to the sound of the morning worship service, he will surely be inspired.”

Mrs. Park invited Chairwoman Kwon and Mr. Willi to visit Korea. It was an invitation that Author Jeong himself had long hoped to extend. The reason was simple: the scene of Mr. Willi hearing the dawn morning worship service at Songgwang-sa—a sound like Eastern music, a sound that seemed to summon the light itself—and drawing inspiration from it deserved to bring Montafon Moonlight to its close. The majestic meeting, in both description and narrative, of Mr. Willi’s Western music, shaped by a Christian worldview, and the Eastern music of the Buddhist morning worship service would embody the metaphorical message that Montafon Moonlight had been seeking all along: freedom and peace, consolation and happiness, serenity and joy. Before leaving the country café, Author Jeong spoke to Chairwoman Kwon almost as if making a personal request.

“I sincerely hope that Mr. Willi’s Jeong will receive its premiere in Hiroshima. Hiroshima is the symbolic city of mercilessness, the place upon which the atomic bomb fell. If people are to recover compassion, then Jeong must be performed there.”

“Please don’t worry. Mr. Willi is already in communication with conductors in Japan. On the day Jeong receives its premiere in Hiroshima, I will be sure to invite you and Mrs. Park.”

“Chairwoman Kwon, if a Japanese premiere proves difficult, then a city in Iran or Ukraine, where the scars of war remain deep, would also be fitting. It should be premiered in such a place, where it can bring comfort to wounded souls. Jeong is that kind of composition.”

“Thank you for your advice, Author Jeong. And regarding the matter we have been hoping for, we will meet with Christoph soon and see if we can move it forward in earnest.”

By then, the Danube and the city of Vienna seemed to be bowing in the deepest reverence beneath a sky washed with crimson sunset. It was the hour when the light of day willingly yielded its place to the coming night and quietly withdrew. Perhaps moved by the sacred atmosphere that lingered at the edge of the day, Chairwoman Kwon spoke in a low voice.

“For some reason, I find myself wanting to pray. For someone, that is. Author Jeong, I will send a car to your lodging by two o’clock tomorrow afternoon.”

“Thank you. Please tell Mr. Willi that I sincerely hope he will come to Korea with you, Chairwoman Kwon.”

In order to catch their night flight, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park would have to leave their lodging for Vienna International Airport at two o’clock that afternoon. As on their journey to Austria, the aircraft would avoid the war-torn skies over Ukraine, turn south toward the Black Sea, and then cross the Asian continent through the night. Such was war, the monster capable of altering even the pathways of the sky. Suddenly, Author Jeong found himself praying that Mr. Willi’s Jeong might be performed somewhere, anywhere, as soon as possible. If the earth were allowed to become a battlefield, it would be laid waste like a desert. And there was no time left for people to cross over into that Pure Land where all were meant to live together in peace.

The End
