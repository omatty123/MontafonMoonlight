Around eleven in the morning, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park went once again to Mr. Willi’s house. They went later than on previous mornings because Mr. Willi had been considerate, thinking Author Jeong’s party must be tired from having explored such places as the log house where he had composed Encounter and the deep Montafon valley that had inspired his opera Brother of Sleep. But perhaps because Author Jeong and Mrs. Park had breathed Montafon’s pure air, they did not feel tired in the least.

Author Jeong, Mrs. Park, and Chairwoman Kwon entered the living room of Mr. Willi’s house. As soon as they sat down, Mr. Willi suggested they go to Bodensee (Lake Constance), the largest lake in central Europe. He had already changed into outdoor clothes. He was dressed formally, though without a tie. It was the first time they had seen him in a suit since meeting him in 2015.

“You aren’t wearing a jumper today.”

Mr. Willi usually wore either a gray or red jumper with jeans. He spoke again.

“I am thinking of going with you and Mrs. Park to the ‘Baroque Concert’ at the Basilica Maria Bildstein in Bregenz, which begins at five this afternoon. The performance will be by Vienna’s city orchestra, the Wiener Symphoniker. While the Vienna Philharmonic is a conservative orchestra that mainly plays the classical repertoire, the Wiener Symphoniker is an orchestra broadening its range of performance, approaching modern music and even K-pop in a popular way.”

Mrs. Park, a classical-music enthusiast, was delighted.

“So that is why Chairwoman Kwon told us to come dressed formally. Thanks to you, Mr. Willi, we will be able to enjoy a Baroque concert and I’m sure it will leave us very happy. So, you must have been invited as an honored guest.”

“At every Bregenz Festival, the Wiener Symphoniker has generally been in charge of the music. Of course, my own inyeon is deep with the Vienna Philharmonic. But during the Bregenz Festival, I, too, have often advised on matters of music.”

Then, quite suddenly, Mr. Willi asked Chairwoman Kwon whether she could translate The Fool Novice into German and read it aloud.

“It is because I do not know Hangeul. You said <The Fool Novice> was written to comment on the importance of silence, didn’t you? I believe I have told you this, but absolute silence is the same as absolute stillness, so I would like to hear it once more in German.”

“I will translate into German and read aloud only the part I read quickly before.”

Chairwoman Kwon opened The Fool Novice, which lay on the living-room table. She slowly passed over the pages she had already read and then began to read aloud in German.

After Chairwoman Kwon finished reading The Fool Novice in German, Mr. Willi smiled. Even as he drove his own car toward Bodensee, he wore a happy expression, still moved by the lingering message of The Fool Novice. Bodensee was like a great sea touching Austria, Germany, and Switzerland. Passenger boats were crossing the lake. There was a floating opera stage on the water, and along the lakeshore were installed thousands of seats for spectators. But perhaps because the opera performance was on another day, the floating stage stood empty.

After a late lunch at a restaurant by the lakeshore, the party moved to the mountainside village of Bildstein in the city of Bregenz. They were going to meet Mr. Willi’s friend, the businessman, editor, and CEO Christoph Ganahl. His company building faced the Basilika Maria-Bildstein, and the view was very fine. The city of Bregenz and Bodensee could be seen at a glance.

On the wall of his office hung a facsimile of a musical score Mr. Willi had drawn by hand, as if it were a work of hyperrealism. On another wall was an abstract-style painting by Max Weiler, whom Christoph said he loved and respected most.

‘So Christoph the businessman loves Willi’s music and Max Weiler’s painting.’

‘Christoph, who loves art, reveres Willi and Max Weiler!’

Chairwoman Kwon introduced Author Jeong to Christoph, who had an intelligent look about him. And when she introduced Mrs. Park, Author Jeong showed him photographs of her ceramic works saved on his phone. Christoph was startled and exclaimed again and again.

“Wunderbar! Wunderbar!”

Author Jeong had shown them because Christoph loved art, and he thought Mrs. Park’s ceramics, too, would meet with his favor. When five o’clock arrived, the people waiting outside the basilica began to enter one by one. Author Jeong’s party also entered, showing the tickets Christoph’s wife had reserved. Inside the basilica, the first floor, the second floor, and even the area directly before the stage filled at once, with not a gap to be found. At last, the concert began. But the conductor’s introduction of honored guests, including Bregenz Mayor Michael Ritsch and Mr. Willi, was so long it grew tedious, while the performance itself was brief, only about an hour and a half. The encore, which began after loud applause, was familiar even to Author Jeong, though he knew little of classical music: a Bach unaccompanied cello suite that seemed to pour sunlight down upon his head. As they left the basilica, Author Jeong said to Chairwoman Kwon,

“It feels like a song of encouragement, breathing life into our Austrian journey.”

“This worked out well. Christoph and his wife say they will buy us dinner, too. The restaurant is above the basilica.”

Author Jeong exchanged business cards with the mayor. Of course, the mayor and Mr. Willi already knew each other, so they only shook hands warmly. When Author Jeong’s party stood to leave after the meal, the mayor came over first and playfully offered his hand, saying, “Chanju Jeong!” Seeing that he had memorized Author Jeong’s name so quickly, Author Jeong thought a politician’s sociability was much the same in Korea and Austria. At that moment, grateful to Mr. Willi for the kindness he had shown all afternoon, Author Jeong murmured to himself.

‘I do not know what inyeon Mr. Willi and I had in a previous life. He gave us his whole afternoon, showed us Bodensee, and let us enjoy a concert in the basilica. How could I be anything but grateful? If only I possessed the Buddha-eye, as the Buddha does, I could see Mr. Willi’s past lives clearly!’

The darkness of Bregenz night was soft as a caressing hand. The lights of the Bregenz streets seemed like starlight descended from the sky. From the direction of Bodensee, a warm wind blew in gently.
