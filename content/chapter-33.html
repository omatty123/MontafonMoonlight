July 30, 2025.

Author Jeong and his wife arrived in Vienna and spent the night in Unit 401 of a six-story mixed-use apartment building attached to a hospital. Chairwoman Kwon said she had borrowed an apartment owned by an acquaintance and handed them the keys. There were two keys: one for the door to Unit 401 and the other for the main entrance to the apartment building. Unit 401 in that building was a place where the couple could stay and come and go as they pleased during their two-week stay in Austria.

The lodging was close to a tram stop, and Schubert's birthplace was nearby. They had also stopped there once when they came in May 2018. Schubert did not seem to be especially popular among Austrians. That time as well, it had been around ten in the morning, but the entrance was locked, and after knocking they had barely managed to get in, only to find that they were the only visitors.

Chairwoman Kwon said they could take the tram, but suggested that they walk into central Vienna and revive old memories. Since they had last come in 2018, it had truly been seven years. Yet before long, their forgotten memories of the city streets returned. Vienna had an unusually large number of churches. At eight in the morning they heard the clear sound of bells ringing from a church near the lodging, and cathedrals often came into view. They even went inside St. Stephen's Cathedral, one of Vienna's landmarks, and breathed in the sacred atmosphere unique to such a church. Chairwoman Kwon then explained why Vienna had so many churches.

"Viennese citizens pay a church tax. That is how the great cathedrals can be maintained. Of course, there is nothing like the tithe offerings of Korean churches, but some believers do make donations to churches according to their own wishes."

Though it was July, the weather was cool, like autumn in Korea. The party walked through central Vienna as if on a leisurely stroll. They passed the Vienna Musikverein Golden Hall and the Vienna Konzerthaus, where Mr. Willi had premiered his own works. Before long they went into Café Imperial, which Mr. Willi had used as a meeting place with notable figures in the music world, and had tea there. Chairwoman Kwon said that Professor Sigmund Freud, who had deeply illuminated the world of the unconscious that Author Jeong so admired, had also been fond of Café Imperial.

"They say Professor Freud came here often. His birthplace is nearby."

Vienna was also a city of cafés. Whichever street they walked down, there were old-fashioned cafés with long histories. Chairwoman Kwon explained them one by one like a guide.

"Among the old cafés, Café Schwarzenberg and Café Demel are representative examples. Tomorrow let us go to Café Demel and have tea."

"Since we had tea today at the Hotel Imperial café, that would be a good place for tomorrow."

They had lunch at Sura, a restaurant run by a Korean. The sign was in Hangul, so it caught the couple's attention even more. But then something happened that surprised them. A Korean expatriate named Kang Seonduk, who had borrowed and been deeply moved by Author Jeong's books from Chairwoman Kwon, had already paid in advance for three days' worth of lunches. Chairwoman Kwon said Kang had worked for more than twenty years at a hospital in Austria before retiring as a head nurse, and that her hometown was in Jeolla Province. Not only that, but Ms. Kang's younger brother, an Oriental medicine doctor, had sent along pills of gyeong ok go, saying they were good for recovering from travel fatigue, and through Chairwoman Kwon they were presented as a gift to Author Jeong. On the spot, Author Jeong handed Chairwoman Kwon two folding fans as gifts.

"These are folding fans I received by asking painter Kim Yangsu for them before coming from Korea. One is for you, Chairwoman Kwon, and please give the other one to Ms. Kang Seonduk."

The fan given to Chairwoman Kwon was painted with a scene of azaleas in full bloom. Author Jeong found himself admiring Koreans like Ms. Kang Seonduk who had come to Austria and in their own ways made successes of themselves.

The next day.

At around ten in the morning, Chairwoman Kwon came again to the lodging, and they decided to walk through Vienna. It was only on the second day that the name of the church nearest the lodging finally became familiar to them: Votiv Church. The nearby park and café also bore the name Votiv.

The party passed Votiv Church and went to City Hall. Hearing what Chairwoman Kwon said, Author Jeong and his wife felt a twinge of envy.

"Among the mayor's policies, the first is arts policy. Living here, I always sympathize with the mayor's policies."

The City Hall building, fitting for a city of churches, had been designed in a church style. A traveler might easily mistake it for a cathedral. Yet the square in front of City Hall resembled a performance venue. A giant screen and fixed seating had been installed there.

"How enviable. It truly is a city of the arts."

"From the last Friday of July, when students begin their vacation, through August, and also on special occasions, they show orchestral performances, opera, vocal music, theater, jazz, musicals, and even vanishing Austrian circus acts free of charge at night. Citizens gather in enormous crowds to watch."

"At Seoul City Hall Plaza or Gwanghwamun Plaza in Korea, there are demonstrations and rallies that cause serious traffic congestion, so it is enviable to hear of citizens gathering instead to enjoy the arts."

Chairwoman Kwon next led the couple to Café Demel, where Mr. Willi had often gone to meet people connected with the music world. But when they saw people lined up waiting, they simply passed by. Author Jeong said,

"Mr. Willi seems to like old cafés."

"Actually, Mr. Willi told me to show you the cafés he used to frequent."

"For what reason?"

"I think he wanted to help with your writing."

This time they went into Café Schwarzenberg. Chairwoman Kwon entered first and found them a seat. The outdoor tables in front of the building were already full. Inside the café it was a little dim, but there was no helping it. At the table right next to them, a middle-aged woman who looked like a professor was quietly tutoring a young student in the basics of vocal music.

"This café, too, was a place where Mr. Willi would meet conductors and performers and discuss performances."

"Then the café manager must know Mr. Willi well."

"Of course. Mr. Willi is a well-known figure in Vienna."

According to Chairwoman Kwon, Café Schwarzenberg had opened in 1861. Later, in 1902, it took the name Café Schwarzenberg, and it served mainly as a meeting place for people in the financial world. She said that the architect Josef Hoffmann had been a regular patron, though to be honest the architect's name meant little to Author Jeong.

In any case, for two days now, the party had been leaving the lodging around ten in the morning and spending the entire afternoon wandering through the streets and alleys of Vienna. If they had walked five or six hours in Seoul, they would have been badly stressed by exhaust fumes and noise, but in Vienna there was far less of that kind of fatigue.

"Even though we have walked quite a distance since morning, I do not feel tired."

"That must be because of your artistic sensibility, Mr. Jeong."

"Just as there are forest paths in the mountains, Vienna seems to have forest paths of music. There is clearly something in Vienna that keeps a person from growing weary."

Mrs. Park, who loved classical music, joined the conversation.

"Johann Strauss, Mozart, Wagner, Schubert, Beethoven, Klimt, Schiller, Freud, and Mr. Willi all seem as though they must have drawn inspiration from Vienna's streets and cafés."

Before they knew it, the party had walked as far as the square in front of Parliament. There, young people who looked to be Iranian students were holding placards and demonstrating against what they described as Israel's atrocities. The party could not simply remain indifferent, so for a moment they offered silent support. It seemed to be a protest that had been properly reported to the police. Only one male officer and one female officer stood nearby watching, and the demonstration was entirely peaceful. The method of protest was to bring out aluminum pots and frying pans to bang on with spoons and chopsticks to make metallic noise. Mrs. Park asked Author Jeong,

"Why did they bring pots with them?"

"Perhaps it is a protest telling Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu not to take away their right to survive."

In the square across from them stood the statue of Empress Maria Theresa of the Habsburg Empire that had once dominated Europe. Come to think of it, Vienna was a city of statues no less than of churches. There were statues standing in every park and square. Even during their walk, the party had already seen statues of Mozart, Goethe, Schiller, Emperor Franz Joseph I, and many others.

The party sat for a while on a bench in the courtyard of a theater building built by Emperor Franz Joseph I, where Mr. Willi's Brother of Sleep had been performed many times, and rested. After that they went as far as the front of the Theater an der Wien, where Mr. Willi's work had been premiered. Chairwoman Kwon explained that it was a historic theater opened in 1801, where Beethoven had been invited to serve as music director, had premiered his Fidelio, and had staged his major works. The doors were locked, perhaps because it was closed, so Author Jeong and his wife could not go inside.

Since the next day they would have to ride the train for six hours to Bregenz Station in order to meet Mr. Willi, the party headed back to the lodging a little earlier than the day before.

"Mr. Willi is waiting so eagerly his eyes are about to fall out."

In response to Chairwoman Kwon's remark, Author Jeong joked,

"We'll go and check if his eyes really popped out or not. Hahaha."

Instead of walking as they had the day before, the party took the tram. Chairwoman Kwon got off first in front of her lodging near the building where Beethoven had died. Author Jeong and his wife got off at a stop just past Schubert's birthplace.
