To go to the Haus für Mozart, the party stood in front of the passenger car Silvia would drive. Even Silvia’s dog Vito came along. On Chairwoman Kwon’s advice, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park had changed out of their casual clothes and into formal dress before taking their seats in the back. They were leaving the hotel at 6:15 p.m., and since the concert began at eight, there was no need to hurry too much. But Salzburg’s city streets were already packed with cars, one after another.

It was only around seven that the party reached the area near the Haus für Mozart. Police officers were thoroughly controlling the roads around the concert hall. Only cars issued a pass could approach the hall itself. Silvia said,

“The aristocrats attending the concert must already have received their passes.”

“We’ll walk a little from here. You can let us out here.”

Vito whined, as if refusing to get out on a street crowded with people. Silvia warned him,

“Vito, you’re not getting out. Be quiet.”

Mrs. Park said,

“That is the name of the mafia boss in the film The Godfather. The police may get nervous because of Vito’s name.”

“Hahaha, for Vito’s sake too, he’s not getting out.”

Silvia had no pass, but she used her wits to take Author Jeong and Mrs. Park all the way to the concert hall entrance. She stopped the car beside a police officer. When he approached, Silvia said confidently,

“I have brought important guests from Korea.”

At that, the officer glanced at Author Jeong and Mrs. Park, seated in the back in formal clothes, then pointed the traffic baton in his hand toward the concert hall entrance. It was a sign permitting them to pass. Even so, the officer tilted his head with a curious expression. In an instant, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park had become important figures from Korea. As she dropped them at the concert hall entrance, Silvia said,

“Hahaha. I did not lie. Author Jeong and Mrs. Park are honored guests who have come from Korea.”

“Silvia did not lie. We will wait at a nearby café and come back when the concert is nearly over.”

The concert was called the Camerata Salzburg Concert. The seats, going up three floors, were already full. A female concert hall staff member noticed Author Jeong and Mrs. Park hesitating because they could not find their seats, and kindly showed them to their assigned places. Their seats were near the front, close enough to see even the expressions of the performers in detail.

The concert began on time, exactly as printed on the ticket. Part One began with an ensemble made up only of violins and cellos playing Vivaldi’s The Four Seasons, familiar even to Author Jeong’s ear. The performers played the entire work with passion for some forty-five minutes, without the slightest looseness. In particular, the concerto-like exchange between the female violinist in a dress the color of a black rose and the male violinist who also served as conductor was more than enough to hold the eye. The Dutch guest violinist Janine Jansen, after the first part ended, gave an encore with movements full of force and received great applause from the audience. If there was one flaw, it was that women who seemed to be classical-music planners were chatting quietly in the seats directly in front of them. It was only natural that Mrs. Park furrowed her brow. By contrast, the mustached, middle-aged Japanese man seated beside them wore a dazzlingly patterned top, as if he were an avant-garde artist, and when Janine Jansen returned to the stage for her encore, he cried out “Bravo!” again and again.

When Part One and the encore ended, the audience was given an intermission. As the central doors of the concert hall opened wide, people poured out into the square. At that moment, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park thought the concert was over and called Chairwoman Kwon. But Chairwoman Kwon did not answer. Sensing that something about the atmosphere was strange, Mrs. Park said,

“Dear, the audience is not scattering. They are gathered here and there in the square, drinking wine.”

“You’re right. Let’s ask one of the concert hall staff.”

Mrs. Park asked one of the female staff members,

“Is the performance over?”

“Yes, the one-hour performance has ended.”

The audience was still lingering in the square, talking together. Yet the staff member’s answer was not exactly wrong. The first part, lasting one hour, had indeed ended. Just then, a small bell suddenly rang. It was the signal that intermission was over and the audience should reenter the hall. People surged back inside. Author Jeong and Mrs. Park, still unfamiliar with the location of the concert hall doors, were again guided by a staff member back to their assigned seats.

Part Two, led by conductor Gregory Ahss, had changed into the scale of a small orchestra. The piece for the second part was Mendelssohn’s Italian Symphony, vivid and alive like brilliant sunlight. The performers played with all their strength, wearing happy expressions as they did. Just seeing the happiness on their faces made Author Jeong feel as though he were being served music. For two and a half hours across Parts One and Two, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park were moved by the performers’ playing and by the joy in their expressions. Their minds were perfectly clear, as if purified by the melodies of the music. Author Jeong felt newly grateful to Chairwoman Kwon. She had barely managed to secure two tickets when the concert was nearly sold out; one ticket cost 193 euros, so two came to more than 670,000 Korean won.

Author Jeong thought of his inyeon with Chairwoman Kwon. It had been a piece of good fortune for him that she, a Catholic, had been captivated by his Buddhist works. As Chairwoman Kwon had passed in and out of Incheon International Airport, she had bought one of Author Jeong’s novels or essay collections at the airport bookstore each time and read it. Once she had even shown him a notebook in which she had packed nearly an entire page with the titles of those books. One day she introduced Author Jeong’s literary world to Mr. Willi, and because Mr. Willi then wanted to meet him, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park had traveled twice to Montafon, where Mr. Willi lived.

When they came out of the concert hall, Silvia and Chairwoman Kwon were waving amid the bustling crowd. Silvia’s car must have been parked somewhere beside Hohensalzburg Fortress. They would have to walk there. In the square near the concert hall, a large screen had been set up. It was a screen meant to show the live performance to citizens who could not afford tickets. As the party passed by, Mozart’s final opera, The Magic Flute, completed only two months before his death, was playing on the screen like a film. Silvia said,

“The greatest reason The Magic Flute is loved beyond its age is, without question, the music. It is full of famous pieces that even people who know little about classical music recognize as soon as they hear them. Among them, the Queen of the Night aria stands out for its tremendous high notes and technique, as if testing the limits of the human voice. It is a cold, furious song in which she commands her daughter to kill Sarastro.”

When they returned to the Romantik Spa Hotel, it was eleven at night. They had planned to see the Mozart Residence that day, but once again they had postponed it. So the next morning they would have to stop by the Mozart Residence and then leave straight for Vienna. Mrs. Park’s flight ticket for Korea had already been booked. Before going to sleep, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park spoke for a while. Author Jeong said,

“Why did Mr. Willi complete Mozart’s unfinished score himself and then hope that two hundred years later someone else would complete it in another version?”

“Mr. Willi is remarkable, thinking in units of two hundred years over Mozart’s unfinished score.”

“I feel as if Mr. Willi meditates within an eternity that transcends time and space. At least one thing is clear: he is an extraordinary genius of a composer.”

“The aristocrat who possessed Mozart’s unfinished score was no ordinary person either. That aristocrat must have understood Mr. Willi’s music. That must be why he gave Mozart’s unfinished score to Mr. Willi.”

Author Jeong was tired, but he could not fall asleep right away. The afterimage of Hohensalzburg Fortress kept circling in his mind.

“Dear, do you think Mozart knew that Salzburg had been under the sea long ago?”

“Archaeologists would have known such things from sedimentary rock, but Mozart, as a composer, may not have. Still, just as Mr. Willi encountered absolute silence in the primeval forest below Kops Lake and saw music there, Mozart too may have drawn inspiration, without knowing it, from Salzburg’s immense primeval sedimentary rock.”

“There is reason in your inference. The principal players of the Vienna Philharmonic said there was something common to Willi’s music and Mozart’s.”

From a forest whose distance could not be gauged came the sound of a nightingale. The sound was very clear and ringing. It seemed like the voice of a weather forecaster announcing that the night had deepened.

The next day, at 10:10 in the morning, the party set out for the Mozart Residence. About twenty minutes later, they arrived and went straight in. Silvia could not enter because of her dog, Vito. If the interior had a defining feature, it was that Mozart’s life was displayed in pictures. Author Jeong stopped before the portrait of Mozart’s father, whose eyes were fierce. Mozart’s own eyes, too, resembled his father’s and were far from ordinary. According to the exhibition text, Mozart remained in conflict with his father until his early death at a young age; this was the exact opposite of the father-son relationship between Mr. Willi and his father, through which love had passed.

After leaving the Mozart Residence, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park and Chairwoman Kwon hurried out of Salzburg. With Vienna almost before them, they stopped briefly at Silvia’s house. It was so she could leave the visibly exhausted Vito at home. Silvia’s tall husband Boris came out as far as the garden and welcomed them warmly. When he stretched out both hands to pick peaches, saying he would give them to Chairwoman Kwon as a gift, he needed no ladder.

“Vienna is only about thirty minutes away, so why don’t you come in for a drink before you go?”

Chairwoman Kwon laughed again, saying Author Jeong had called Boris a borisu, “Bodhi tree.” But Silvia’s tall husband pointed to the Bodhi tree growing to one side of the yard and said,

“I grow it because its heart-shaped leaves are beautiful.”

When Author Jeong and Mrs. Park kept turning their eyes toward the gate, Silvia said as if to reassure them,

“I will take both of you and Sonja to your lodging, then come right back. Author Jeong, if the two of you ever come to Vienna again, I will drive and guide you then as well.”

Author Jeong expressed his gratitude to Chairwoman Kwon.

“Thanks to you, Chairwoman Kwon, it seems we have come to know kind Mrs. Silvia.”

As if sad to part, Vito circled round and round between Boris’s large legs. Boris stood there like an old tree in the garden, impassive, and waved. In saying goodbye, he showed no more emotion than necessary.
