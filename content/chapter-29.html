It was the morning of their departure from Italy to Austria. As they finished checking out at the resort reception and gathered their luggage, Lionel, a waiter from the restaurant, came running. Over fifteen days of crossing paths, he had grown fond of Author Jeong and his wife. He was the young man who, whenever they ordered, would bring the menu and spend a good ten minutes patiently guiding them through it. Author Jeong gave him his business card. With halting English and expressive eyes, they had shared many warm conversations.

To reach Austria, they followed Mr. Guster's directions: take the train from Follonica station to Roma Trastevere, transfer, and get off at Fiumicino Airport station. They needed about three hours of margin to catch their Lufthansa flight to Vienna. But when they arrived at the airport, the departure time had been delayed and the gate changed twice. Had this happened at Incheon Airport in Korea, voices would have been raised and complaints would have flown. Here, everyone stayed quiet.

At last they boarded, and in the Austrian newspaper tucked into the seat pocket, a photograph of North Korean leader Kim Jong-un dominated the front page. It was proof that even in Europe, the North Korea-U.S. talks drew keen interest. Author Jeong hoped the stalled second round of negotiations would go well.

The flight from Fiumicino to Vienna took just over an hour and a half. Emerging into the arrivals hall, they found Chairwoman Kwon of IMK waiting to greet them. Having visited at her invitation three years earlier, the airport felt familiar. The plan was to spend the night at an annex of Chairwoman Kwon's office on Beethovengasse. The street must have taken its name from the building where Beethoven had died.

The next day, however, they were to visit Krems, a city older than Vienna and similar to Kyŏngju in Korea. Krems lay about an hour's drive from Vienna. Author Jeong and his wife had stopped there three years ago as well. A hillside town clustered around its cathedral. A pastoral city where the Danube glinted in the sunlight beyond the vineyards. Atop the hill stood Stift Göttweig, a monastery designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The night passed like a flash of lightning. Chairwoman Kwon waited outside with the same bright disposition as the day before. They were headed to Dr. Werner's house in Krems. Since they were married, it was of course Chairwoman Kwon's home as well. Dr. Werner had originally been a public physician in the Krems area. After retiring, the local farmers had prevailed upon him to stay, so he settled into a house along the Danube.

Dr. Werner came outside to welcome them.

"Our house was originally monastery land. I'm not sure if we'll run into the priest who granted us this precious plot today."

Author Jeong and his wife went inside for a cup of black tea, then stepped back out to stroll through the garden. After that, they followed Dr. Werner up to Stift Göttweig to see the church. By good fortune, they happened upon Father Maurus, the monastery's chief steward and the very priest who had allotted Dr. Werner his plot of land. The priest had a cast on his foot. He explained:

"Stift Göttweig was founded in 1403, but it burned to the ground and was rebuilt in 1718. Krems is a major white wine region. The soil is a mix of sand and limestone, ideal for growing grapes. Every household here has its own wine cellar. In 2006, Krems celebrated its millennium."

Dr. Werner had a cast on his finger, and Father Maurus had one on his foot. The priest laughed cheerfully, saying they were both wearing casts, "medals of honor earned on the job." Calling injuries medals of honor seemed fitting for such a hardworking priest.

Their next destination was Willendorf, where the Venus figurine had been unearthed. Dr. Werner drove slowly along the Danube, heading in the direction of Germany. On the way to Willendorf, gorges flanked the river, and their hillsides were lined with terraced vineyards. The Venus herself had been discovered while clearing land for a vineyard.

When they arrived at the site, the group first read the information panel about the Venus. The figurine was estimated to have been carved during the Paleolithic era, between 24,900 and 23,900 years ago, and measured roughly eleven centimeters. She had curly hair, her hands resting on large breasts, and her belly swelled like that of a woman in the final stage of pregnancy.

Were voluptuous women the ideal in Paleolithic times? A symbol of fertility and abundance?

But Author Jeong did not want to impose modern interpretations. The figure resembled the ample builds of Western women he had frequently passed on the street.

By the time they returned to Dr. Werner's house, darkness had already fallen. Only the warm sound of the Danube flowing reached them. Perhaps because it was monastery land, the air felt pure. The next day they would head back to Vienna, but the place Author Jeong truly wanted to see was the Egon Schiele Museum. It was in Tulln, a small town between Vienna and Krems, so a visit would not be difficult.

The following day, Chairwoman Kwon took over from Dr. Werner as their guide. Before staying behind in Krems, Dr. Werner presented Author Jeong with a replica of the Austrian Venus figurine. Author Jeong asked Chairwoman Kwon if they could stop at the Schiele Museum on their way to Vienna. Schiele, with his elongated neck, bore a certain resemblance to the Korean poet Yi Sang. The thought of visiting Tulln, the city of Schiele's birth, and encountering his paintings before meeting Mr. Willi set his heart racing. Until high school, Author Jeong had been an aspiring painter in the art club.

The turbulent arc of Schiele's life ran roughly as follows.

Born in Tulln as the son of the stationmaster, Schiele moved with his parents to nearby Krems at the age of eleven and transferred schools. When his father died of syphilis, his maternal uncle, a railroad company employee, became his guardian. Recognizing the boy's talent for drawing, his uncle sent him to the Academy of Fine Arts in Vienna in 1906, at the age of sixteen. But Schiele rebelled against the academy's traditional conservatism and dropped out. As fortune would have it, he met Klimt in 1907 and took him as his mentor. Klimt recognized Schiele's talent, purchased his works, and connected him with patrons. After three years at the Vienna Academy, Schiele joined discontented fellow students in forming the Neukunstgruppe, a new art group. In 1909, Klimt included Schiele in an exhibition where the young painter encountered the works of Edvard Munch, Jan Toorop, and Vincent van Gogh, experiencing an aesthetic awakening. From that point, Schiele began to explore not only the human form but human sexuality. He first participated in group shows in cities like Prague, Budapest, and Cologne, then from 1913 held solo exhibitions in Munich and Paris. Schiele's private life, as is well known, was troubled. In 1911, he began living with seventeen-year-old Wally in Vienna; she was said to have modeled not only for Schiele but for Klimt as well. They left Vienna for his mother's hometown of Český Krumlov in Bohemia (then known as Krumau), but were driven out when the townspeople denounced him for painting nudes of adolescent girls. Yet Schiele maintained that even erotic works possessed a sanctity. They moved next to a cheap studio in Neulengbach, thirty-five kilometers west of Vienna. There, however, Schiele was arrested on charges of seducing a minor. Police seized hundreds of drawings and paintings, labeling them pornographic, and put him on trial. He served twenty-one days in custody and three days of imprisonment. The judge, who went so far as to burn one of Schiele's drawings in open court, dismissed the charges of seduction and abduction but found him guilty of exhibiting obscene images. Schiele then parted ways with Wally and in 1915 married Edith, the daughter of a locksmith. But the First World War disrupted his work. Just three days after his wedding, he left for Prague to serve in the military. Returning to Vienna in 1917, Schiele devoted himself entirely to painting. Within a year, fifty of his works were featured at a 1918 Vienna exhibition. The exhibition poster caused a sensation: Schiele had parodied The Last Supper, placing himself in the seat of Christ. That was as far as his life would go. Heaven called him home. In 1918, the Spanish flu struck twenty million across Europe. Schiele's wife, six months pregnant, died that autumn, and three days after her death, Schiele closed his eyes at the age of twenty-eight.

Like Dr. Werner's house, the Schiele Museum stood on the banks of the Danube. Perhaps because the town was small, they found it easily. A bronze statue of Schiele stood at the entrance. It was still morning, so there were few visitors. Author Jeong and his wife took in Schiele's works displayed on the first and second floors. The early landscapes and portraits carried an Impressionist quality. But the later works grew darker in tone, asymmetrical, with exaggerated sexual expression. One could call these Expressionist pieces grotesque and decadent. But could such a judgment be the final word on paintings that projected a melancholy genius's inner world without pretense? Leopold, who owned more of Schiele's works than anyone and understood him most truly, had called Schiele "the eternal child," declaring: "What is different about a child's way of seeing? Children's drawings are a reflection of reality." How difficult it is to live in this world and remain a child, free of prejudice and preconception! Author Jeong and his wife learned that the Leopold Museum in Vienna was holding an exhibition commemorating the centennial of Klimt's and Schiele's deaths. Their timing in visiting Austria, they felt, could not have been better.
