It was the first time since coming to Montafon that they had visited the church with Mr. Willi around nine in the morning. St. Agatha Church stood by the road only fifty meters uphill from the lodging where Author Jeong and Mrs. Park were staying. The party went inside the church, and Mr. Willi said,

“During the war, the church building was destroyed, but the villagers had moved the sacred objects inside to safety beforehand, so they escaped damage.”

“By the war, you mean the Second World War, I suppose.”

“Yes. Both of my parents were war orphans.”

“That splendid processional cross is a treasure more than seven hundred years old.”

On an information board outside the church, an explanation of the Romanesque processional cross was written in German and Chairwoman Kwon interpreted it into Korean for them.

“One of the most important artistic and cultural treasures in Vorarlberg, this late Romanesque processional cross was made around 1225 to 1250. It was produced in a workshop in Limoges in southwest France, with gilded copper plates over a wooden core, enamel coloring, and jewel ornamentation. After an attempted theft in 1977, the cross was finally moved to a secure place for safekeeping. For several years now, a copy made by Beatrice Pfeifer has been on display.”

Outside the church was the cemetery where the villagers were buried, yet it did not look bleak; it looked warm and gentle. When Mr. Willi told him the height of the silver-colored mountain in front, the one he had seen through their lodging window, Author Jeong was more than a little surprised.

“Author Jeong, the rocky face of that peak alone rises a thousand meters. When I was about ten, in winter I used to follow my father up to that summit and then ski down. If I had not fallen ill, I might have become a skier.”

Mr. Willi's father appeared now and then in his stories, so the jeong between father and son felt unusually deep. The party got into Mr. Willi's car and headed for Kops Lake. On the way, they stopped briefly at the birthplace of Franz Josef Rudigier, Bishop of Linz, who had been close enough to Anton Bruckner to hear his confession. They got out of the car and, in the empty yard of the birthplace, stretched their arms and legs as if doing warm-up exercises. The bishop's birthplace stood in the last village on the mountain road up to Kops Lake.

The reason Mr. Willi had brought them to Kops Lake was clear. Standing on the Kops Lake dam and looking down toward the primeval forest below, he said,

“When I look at that primeval forest below the dam, I feel absolute silence. It is a place where one can experience absolute silence.”

“So, this must be that primeval absolute silence untouched by the noise of the secular world.”

To Author Jeong’s eyes, too, the primeval forest seemed like a center into which the energies of the surrounding peaks gathered. Since he was scheduled to leave Montafon the next day, Author Jeong had resolved to ask as many questions as he could. What he most wanted to know was what sounds Mr. Willi heard from all these different parts of nature. Without hesitating, Author Jeong asked straightaway,

“What sound comes from the rocky peak across the lake?”

“That mountain seems to be rooted deep in the earth, just as its crystal-like rock rises high above it.”

Mr. Willi closed his eyes and for a moment took the posture of listening to the mountain’s sound. Then he said seriously,

“I hear the sound of a contrabass as large as the three-story building that manages the lake water.”

The contrabass is the largest of the string instruments, giving a weighty, low register; but when Mr. Willi said it sounded like a contrabass as large as the three-story lake-management building, Author Jeong could not even imagine it. Then Author Jeong pointed to a wildflower blooming along the side path to the lake dam, a red willowherb flower, and asked,

“What sound comes from that wildflower?”

“It makes a sound lower than the low register of an oboe, and also the clean, clear sound of a clarinet.”

“You said, Mr. Willi, that you could express the sound you just heard with colored pencils, didn’t you?”

“Yes. That is why I used to carry colored pencils with me.”

“It is marvelous that sound can be transformed into color.”

“The oboe and clarinet are also instruments shepherds have carried and played since long ago.”

“Now that I hear you say that, the wild willowherb flower does seem to resemble a shepherd who once passed through here.”

Mr. Willi returned to the absolute silence he had spoken of a little earlier.

“When I walked through Montafon’s primeval forests, I felt absolute silence, and I realized that absolute silence also existed within me. It was only that my desires, afflictions, and stray thoughts prevented it from revealing itself.”

“This is the same principle in Buddhism, where the Buddha-nature (佛性) present in the other living beings we see also exists within oneself.”

“In order to wash away the desires, afflictions, and stray thoughts covering my inner being, I came often to Montafon’s primeval forests and walked and walked. So my music begins from primeval absolute silence.”

Author Jeong understood Mr. Willi’s words. When he tried substituting emptiness (gong 空) for absolute silence, understanding came more quickly. Absolute silence was not mere absolute stillness; just as music was full within it, emptiness, too, was not simply a vacant state. Jingong Myoyu (眞空妙有)—True Emptiness, Wondrous Being. It was empty, yet full of wisdom and Buddha-nature (佛性); that state was emptiness. To put it in numbers, zero lay between 1 and -1. Zero was emptiness, something no one could explain as any particular thing, yet it was not nothing at all, because it was something that existed between 1 and -1.

After finishing their conversation about absolute silence at Kops Lake, the party went deeper into the mountains. Because Mr. Willi had obtained an entry permit in advance, they could go farther into the Alps.

“From here on, landslide danger is ever-present, so one cannot go in without a permit.”

As the car drove on, three or four lakes smaller than Kops Lake appeared.

“This part of Montafon is the middle zone of the Alps.”

“To think I am here in the Alps—it moves me deeply.”

“I used to come here deliberately and wait until the hikers and cycling groups had disappeared, then enter absolute silence for hours and receive inspiration.”

The party went into a restaurant overlooking a small lake and ate lunch. During the meal, Author Jeong again pointed to the valley water flowing down in white streams along the foothills of the Alps and asked,

“What sound comes from that place over there?”

“When two notes on a piano are struck at the same time, there are moments when an evil sound like the Devil’s voice arises. But the valley water has none of that sound at all; it has the energy of water that washes away anguish and affliction. It makes a sound like the bows of violins and other string instruments just barely touching the strings.”

Then, quite naturally, Mr. Willi began to speak of his symphony Jeong (情).

“I used the sound of that valley water in the second and sixth movements of Jeong. Absolute silence also appears repeatedly, and the theme of the tenth movement of Jeong is absolute peace and absolute freedom, the happiness and joy in which anything can be fulfilled.”

In 2018, at Mr. Willi’s office in the Wiener Konzerthaus, Author Jeong had explained to him the Buddhist Ten Oxherding Pictures, which depict truth in ten stages by using an ox as a metaphor, and Willi had not forgotten it. That was because the ten-movement progression of Jeong was quite similar to the Ten Oxherding Pictures. In particular, the theme of the final, tenth stage was the same in meaning in Jeong and in the Ten Oxherding Pictures.

After lunch, the party returned to their lodging. Each would rest in their room, then meet again at Mr. Willi’s house at seven that evening. They had made the arrangement because Mr. Willi said he had something he wanted to tell them.

Sure enough, around seven that evening, when Author Jeong and Mrs. Park and Chairwoman Kwon entered his living room, Mr. Willi briefly explained the four works in the Montafon series he had composed. The first was a concerto for trumpet and orchestra, titled Eirene, and he said its theme was the joy and peace of life. He added that he had composed it in 3.5-movement form, thinking of his younger sister Irene, who had died while hiking alone in the Montafon mountains. The first and second movements gather people for a journey and reach the third movement, while the remaining 0.5 movement is a piece in which they turn back before arriving at their destination. Chairwoman Kwon said,

“They say the search party looking for the missing hiker searched every corner, but in the end they could not find Irene. In the end, Irene could not return home, and Mr. Willi composed the piece while meditating on his younger sister.”

At Chairwoman Kwon’s words, Mrs. Park said one thing.

“Seen that way, mountains are compassionate sages, but at times perhaps they are not.”

The second work was a concerto for flute, oboe, and orchestra, titled Geraume Zeit, meaning a waiting room between life and death. Mr. Willi had been contacted by a doctor he knew who lived about four hundred meters from his house. The doctor told him that his wife, Monica, seemed to have only a few days left to live and wanted to see Mr. Willi’s score. Monica had always loved Mr. Willi’s music very much. At this, Author Jeong said,

“In Buddhism, too, there is a world called the intermediate state between life and death. It is said that when a person dies, they remain in the intermediate realm for forty-nine days before going to the Pure Land or to hell.”

“The theme of the third movement of the second piece is love, ‘the Creator who is always with you.’”

Mr. Willi always called the one God of Christianity the Creator. It was because he believed that people’s pure faith in God had become polluted and corrupted.

The third work was a concerto for clarinet and orchestra, titled Ego eimi, meaning “I am,” a declarative phrase Jesus uses when revealing his divinity and identity. The theme of the work, he said, was “absolute silence before sound, and the resonance of being, the music, that emerges from within that absolute silence.” The fourth work was a concerto for horn and orchestra titled Aon, meaning an aeon, an infinite span of time, and he explained that its theme was the “timeless eternity” felt within Alpine nature.

“I composed the fourth movement of the fourth work by referring to the words of a priest. That priest always told me to look at the steps I was taking.”

“The priest’s words are interesting. In Buddhism, too, monks tell us to practice jogo gakha (照顧脚下). It means to look always beneath your own feet. May I understand it that way?”

“Of course.”

When difficult Buddhist terms appeared, Chairwoman Kwon interpreted haltingly, and Mrs. Park repeatedly checked the conversation between Mr. Willi and Author Jeong until she understood it herself, then recorded it in her notebook. And yet Mr. Willi and Author Jeong, even when Chairwoman Kwon’s interpretation was not perfect, had already begun, from some point on, to communicate naturally.
