Author Jeong now felt that Mr. Willi was like a friend. The many conversations they had exchanged the day before had given him a fair understanding of Mr. Willi's music. When Author Jeong mentioned that he would work toward arranging the premiere of Jeong at Panmunjom in Korea, Mr. Willi's face lit up with a smile of pleasure.

"It was when I stopped in Korea after watching the New Japan Philharmonic Orchestra give the premiere of my concerto in Tokyo. While traveling through Korea, lecturing and debating with fresh-faced students at the Korean National University of Arts, visiting Haein-sa, seeing Ihwajang where Mrs. Francesca had lived in Seoul, I heard three sounds. They were the highest pitch, the lowest pitch, and the longest tone I have ever perceived."

Author Jeong ventured his own interpretation of the three sounds Mr. Willi had heard in Korea.

"The highest pitch must be Korean heung, exuberance. The lowest must be Korean han, grief refined into resilience. And the longest must be Korean jeong."

"Why do you say that?"

"Since ancient times, Koreans have loved song, dance, and poetry. It is because they possess an abundance of heung. Korea has also endured countless invasions from China, Japan, and others, and so carries deep sorrow. Yet Koreans have a strength that overcomes sorrow through sorrow itself. That strength is called han. And perhaps because of that arduous history of foreign invasion, Koreans also possess a heart that helps neighbors without condition. That heart is called jeong. Heung is a joyful sound, so it cannot help being high. Han is tempered deep within the heart, so it cannot help being low. And jeong, which has no end, cannot help being long."

"Ah, I see. Through the Jeong I have composed, I want people to feel that the human being and the universe are one. Each movement will offer a small taste of jeong. From the first to the fourth movement, the orchestra plays simultaneously. In the remaining movements, the orchestra is joined first by a soloist, then by two performers."

"Are you thinking of including performers from both North and South Korea in the section where two players join the orchestra?"

"Exactly. Because Korea is divided between North and South, I would love it if both sides performed Jeong together in pursuit of peace and freedom. One possibility would be to split the orchestra members half and half."

Mr. Willi was well versed in the history of the two Koreas. He understood that before 1945, although it was under Japanese colonial rule, Korea had been a single nation, and that it was divided by the United States and the Soviet Union.

"After 1945 came the division. The Soviet Union and the United States swiftly drew the 38th parallel. In the South, the Syngman Rhee regime was established with American military support as a democratic government; in the North, the Kim Il-sung regime was built with Soviet backing as a communist government. In short, the great powers decided everything as they pleased, without consulting Korea, a small and powerless nation."

"I do hope that Jeong will be performed at Panmunjom."

"I will do my best when I return to Korea, though I cannot promise anything. Relations between North and South have been a cycle of tension repeated again and again."

Relations between the two Koreas were like the weather, alternating between overcast and clear. Since the spring of that year, the relationship had been moving toward peace: the third inter-Korean summit was held in April, and North Korean athletes had participated in the Pyeongchang Winter Olympics held in the South. Yet the possibility of renewed conflict between the US and China, or between the US and North Korea, remained ever present, and the state of inter-Korean tension was impossible to predict. Author Jeong changed the subject.

"Mr. Willi, how do you understand the concept of jeong?"

"I understand it as the supreme concept, standing above both Christian love and Buddhist compassion."

"Ha ha ha."

"Is my view mistaken?"

"Not at all. But rather than comparing jeong with other concepts, I would suggest understanding it as something with absolute value in its own right. Nor do I think one needs to regard jeong as something lofty or sacred. Of course, Mr. Willi, the fact that you have expanded the concept of jeong into the philosophical realm is quite inspiring. In any case, jeong is clearly one of the most positive values that humanity possesses. In Korean, the word jeong appears in a remarkable number of compounds: bujeong, a father's affection; mojeong, a mother's affection; ujeong, the affection between friends; and many more. Beyond that, there is 'beautiful jeong,' and there is even 'ugly jeong' — the affection that grows between people through quarreling. So one might also understand jeong through the lens of Hegel's dialectic of thesis, antithesis, and synthesis."

When Author Jeong mentioned Hegel, a light came into Mr. Willi's eyes. In that instant, Author Jeong realized that Mr. Willi knew the philosopher well. His intuition proved correct. Mr. Willi responded at once.

"I am fond of Hegel's dialectic. It astonishes me to learn that Korean has such a thing as 'ugly jeong.' There is no such expression in Europe. How 'ugly' and 'beautiful' can merge into one would be impossible to explain without Hegel's philosophy of thesis, antithesis, and synthesis."

Author Jeong, too, had been drawn to Hegel's philosophy in his university days, and hearing that Mr. Willi felt the same deepened the sense that their minds were in tune. Mr. Willi continued.

"Thirty-five years ago, while walking with my friend Rudi, a priest, in Italy, I told him something. People love to divide things, classical music, modern music, folk music, Eastern music, Western music, and so on. But for me, only one music exists. My music does not belong to any category; it is simply a single mass. For me there is no good music or bad music, only the question of whether it is fused or not. That is why I came to explain my music through Hegel's philosophy. Come to think of it, Jeong may be the very music I have been striving to realize."

Just then, Chairwoman Kwon entered the office. When they had parted the evening before, she had offered to translate for Mr. Willi if Author Jeong would write out "What is jeong?" and "What are the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures?" on A4 paper and bring them. Author Jeong said:

"Chairwoman Kwon, here is the piece I wrote last night. However, I will send the essay on the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures by email after I return to Korea. I want to consult Buddhist reference materials to write it accurately."

"You have done well. Once you send the essay on the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures, I will have my son translate it into English and pass it along to Mr. Willi."

The manuscript Author Jeong handed to Chairwoman Kwon, titled "What is Jeong?", read as follows. It was, of course, a text that reflected Author Jeong's personal perspective.

What is Jeong?

Jeong is the warm and benevolent heart that Koreans possess. Just as the Korean word nim — used to address a truly beloved person — cannot be translated as a mere "you," so too is jeong a word that defies translation. Yet Koreans sense and understand jeong instinctively. How, then, can one explain jeong to a foreigner? Rather than offering a dictionary definition, it seems more effective to reveal, one by one, what I believe to be the essence of jeong.

Jeong has the power to open the doors of the heart. Jeong does not compel, as religion does. Like a flower that blooms where it stands, it simply lets its fragrance be felt.

Jeong is love, a heart that bestows compassion. Jeong asks nothing in return. Like a spring that never runs dry, it simply offers its water to the thirsty.

Jeong is a gift of the heart, both given and received. Joy grows the more jeong is shared. And sorrow shrinks the more jeong is shared.

Jeong does not belong to God. In the Korean language, there is no expression for giving jeong to God. Nor is there any expression for God giving jeong. It exists only among human beings.

Jeong is a heart capable of saving a person. Jeong is the heart that forgives, the heart that considers others, the heart of compassion that makes a human being truly human.

Jeong is a heart capable of bringing peace to the world. Because jeong resides within the human heart, there is no need to seek it elsewhere. It is one of the simplest means by which peace in the world might be achieved.

Jeong is the homeland to which all the people of the world must return. For jeong is like a flower, like a spring, like a special gift; it exists only among human beings; it can save a person; and it is one of the simplest means by which peace in the world might be achieved.

When Chairwoman Kwon translated the piece into English on the spot and read it aloud, Mr. Willi responded as though he grasped it immediately.

"Jeong has the power to open the doors of the heart; it is love and a heart that bestows compassion. It is also a heart capable of bringing peace to the world. It does not belong to God. It exists only among human beings."

"That is correct. You have understood it exactly as I intended."

"I very much look forward to reading the essay on the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures that you will send."

"Yes, I will send it without fail as soon as possible."

A sudden impulse struck Author Jeong like a flash of lightning: he wanted to write Mr. Willi's life in music as a novel. Just as Somerset Maugham had taken the painter Gauguin as the protagonist of The Moon and Sixpence. The very fact that they had met and spoken together was novelistic material, and confidence surged within him. In truth, when he first met Mr. Willi in September 2015, he had told both Chairwoman Kwon and Mr. Willi candidly, "I understand you well enough to want to write about you, but I cannot promise, since it would mean returning to Austria."

At noon, Mr. Willi rose to his feet in a hurry. He needed to catch a train to Innsbruck. Declaring that the time spent with Author Jeong had made him happy, he extended his hand. Author Jeong was equally grateful. He and Mrs. Park followed Mr. Willi down to the entrance of the Wiener Konzerthaus to see him off. There, Author Jeong took two photographs with Mr. Willi, who was dressed in jeans and a red jacket.
