After spending a night at Hotel Bergerhof, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park woke before dawn. The moon was still hanging above the peaks of Montafon, a half moon gone faint in the first light. Through the large window of their room, the mountain before them, one ridge of the Alps, seemed close at hand. It was one of the mountains of the Alpine Montafon valley. Author Jeong, who had risen first, walked to the window and suddenly cried out.

“Sweetheart, look at that mountain!”

“What is it?”

“The painting on the folding fan by Kim Yangsu looks exactly like that mountain.”

The half moon floating above the bluish peaks washed with dawn, and the white stream running almost straight down between the slopes to the right, looked astonishingly like the painting on the folding fan. Author Jeong had never asked painter Kim Yangsu to paint the scenery of the Alpine Montafon, though he knew he would be traveling there, and since the painter depicted Montafon’s moon and mountains without knowing Montafon at all, there was no way to explain it except as inspiration. Besides, was not the title of the novel Author Jeong had already begun serializing Montafon Moonlight? It could only have been an overlap wrought in the unconscious of Author Jeong, Mr. Willi, and painter Kim Yangsu.

Mrs. Park slipped the folding fan into her handbag. Author Jeong was curious to see how Mr. Willi would react when he received it. Chairwoman Kwon, who had already been given a folding fan painted with azaleas in full bloom, might be less surprised. At the appointed hour of nine, Chairwoman Kwon also came down to the hotel lobby. She and Author Jeong and Mrs. Park stepped outside the hotel, where red geraniums were blooming vividly, and waited for Mr. Willi. To the left of the road stood a white church with a small bell tower. Mr. Willi’s house lay down the road to the right. Beyond the cliff on the mountainside was the village of St. Anton, where he lived. After Mr. Willi and Chairwoman Kwon exchanged their morning greetings, he said,

“I’m not sure how the weather will turn out. Montafon weather is very fickle.”

“We’ll be doing nothing but interviews at your house all day, Mr. Willi, so it should be fine.”

Before long, they drove down to St. Anton in Mr. Willi’s car. About ten minutes later they arrived at his house, with its little garden. The party immediately climbed the wooden staircase on the outside up to the second floor. To Author Jeong, the interior felt more familiar than it had in 2015.

The piano on the left side of the hallway leading to the living room was where it had always been. High on the wall opposite the entrance, a new addition caught his eye: an ink orchid painting, a kind of Korean painting. Its Chinese inscription, Dahyang Manri (茶香萬里), meant that the fragrance of tea travels ten thousand li.

On the left side of the living room stood a long cabinet that looked as though it had been made by Mr. Willi’s father, who had also been a carpenter, and on top of it the first thing to catch the eye was the owl ceramic Mrs. Park had given him in 2015. On the wall above the cabinet, the large, dreamlike, surrealist oil painting they had seen before still seemed to welcome Author Jeong and Mrs. Park. On the wall nearby to the right, the figure of Jesus nailed to the cross, carved by Mr. Willi’s grandfather, was still there as well. In the living room and dining room hung two Korean paintings they had not seen before. One showed three pine trees, and the other was a portrait of a beauty in hanbok holding a fan. On the wall right beside the dining-room door there was another figure of Jesus, this one by Mr. Willi’s father, and in the dining room there was also a painting simply propped on the floor, as if there were nowhere to hang it. It showed musicians playing their instruments while a dancing boy performed, recalling Kim Hongdo’s genre paintings. Not only that, in a corner of the dining room there was also a doll of a woman in hanbok playing the geomungo.

Come to think of it, Mr. Willi clearly loved all things Korean. He had many admirers in Japan, yet the paintings and dolls in his house had all come from Korea. As he himself had confessed, perhaps he really had been Korean in a previous life. Perhaps because of the Korean paintings, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park felt at ease. Before beginning the interview, Author Jeong presented Mr. Willi with the folding fan he had brought. He said,

“This is a folding fan I received from painter Kim Yangsu in Korea. But the picture on it is remarkably like the mountains of Montafon. It’s astonishing.”

“Ah, I see. I would like to know more about the painter’s background.”

“He used to teach students at a university, but now he has gone down to his hometown of Jindo and paints full-time. I will ask Chairwoman Kwon to write out his biography for you separately.”

The party sat around the round table in the living room and spent a while taking in the view beyond the large window. The village outside was impeccably clean. Mr. Willi explained that because Europeans came to the Montafon valley, about a thousand meters above sea level, in every season, there were many hotels and guesthouses, and business was always brisk.

Clouds of rain were already hanging on the sharp peaks. The village on the slope across from them appeared and disappeared again beneath avalanche-like banks of mist. It was as though the mountain spirit of Montafon were performing magic for Author Jeong and Mrs. Park. Suddenly Chairwoman Kwon turned to Author Jeong and apologized.

“There is something I must apologize to you for, Mr. Jeong.”

“What is it?”

“It was when I went to Korea in 2017 and visited your home in Hwasun. You gave me a book for Mr. Willi, and you even signed it.”

“Did I? What book was it?”

“It was a very lovely Seon fairy tale, The Fool Novice. But when I returned to Austria, I left it in my room and completely forgot about it. I happened to notice it this time and brought it with me.”

Before handing The Fool Novice to Mr. Willi, Chairwoman Kwon opened to the page Author Jeong had signed and showed it to him. Sure enough, Author Jeong’s inscription appeared as follows.

To Mr. Willi,

A single raindrop is the universe,

and the universe is a single raindrop.

August 4, 2017

From Jeong Chanju

When Chairwoman Kwon translated Author Jeong’s inscription into German for Mr. Willi, he smiled and said,

“These are true words. I agree. My own life in music began, too, with the sound of a single raindrop I heard in the river at Innsbruck.”

Mrs. Park said,

“So Chairwoman Kwon didn’t give Mr. Willi this book even when we came to Vienna in 2018.”

“No, it is even better that Chairwoman Kwon brought it this time. I wrote, ‘A single raindrop is the universe,’ as my own way of interpreting the sound of raindrops Mr. Willi heard in his despair at seventeen. If there is time today, I want to hear that story again, though I have heard it many times already.”

At Mrs. Park’s words, Author Jeong answered that things had turned out for the better. Then Chairwoman Kwon, looking apologetic, said,

“As part of my apology, I will translate The Fool Novice into German and read it aloud here.”

Chairwoman Kwon began to read The Fool Novice slowly and distinctly. Mr. Willi listened, resting his chin on one hand.

‘The temple was already five hundred years old. The pillars of the Dharma hall were deeply lined with wrinkles, and the places where knots had once been had rotted through, leaving holes like a moktak. In spring, tits sometimes raised their young in those holes. There were times when a monk, saying the hall was too noisy, blocked the holes up, but thanks to the child the mother birds would always return to raise their chicks. On bright moonlit nights, the child would secretly open the holes again behind the monks’ backs.

The room where the monks studied was just as worn. Wild grasses sprouted between the broken roof tiles and bloomed. Yellow dandelions flowered here and there, and sometimes purple thistles bloomed. Evening primroses would open their petals in the daytime and yawn, only to be caught by the child.

There were many mice in the ceiling of the monks’ old room as well. Their squeaking could be heard at all hours.

“Squeak, squeak squeeeak squeak.”

At mealtimes the mice made even more noise, because the smell of wild greens, mugwort soup, and pine mushrooms set out in the monks’ bowls spread through the whole ceiling. The monks never fed the mice, not ever. They said the act of stealing food in secret was part of the mice’s karma. They believed that only by erasing that karma of “stealing food,” as if doing homework, could the mice be reborn in bodies better than those of mice. So, sorry as they were, they said they could not help them.

‘They must be hungry. Wouldn’t it be wonderful if, for just one day, we could set a table for the mice.’

At just this point, light came into Mr. Willi’s eyes. After Chairwoman Kwon finished translating and reading the rest of The Fool Novice in German, Author Jeong said,

“The child’s heart is the Buddha’s heart. I believe that your heart, Mr. Willi, is like the heart of the foolish novice in that story.”

Mrs. Park said with a laugh,

“You are a foolish grown-up, Mr. Willi, a foolish composer. My husband is a fool too. He knows nothing but writing. Even now, he doesn’t know how to use a bank card.”

“The title of the storybook is almost chilling. The Fool Novice… Mr. Willi, you may be a foolish novice who insists on silence. Your heart is like that of an innocent child.”

“That is right. I wrote the fairy tale to say how important silence is. In the story, the child’s silence may be the same thing as Mr. Willi’s absolute silence.”

At that moment Chairwoman Kwon brought out something to snack on from the dining room.

“This is a cake our Frida made. It is called a Linzer torte. Linz is the city we passed through yesterday. Linzer torte is said to be the oldest cake in the world, historically speaking.”

Frida was Mr. Willi’s second companion, and Author Jeong and Mrs. Park had met her in 2015. They ate together at a Thai restaurant, and she was the woman with a son who had not been able to join them for the Montafon folk play because of work. Chairwoman Kwon said that when Frida heard Author Jeong and Mrs. Park were coming to Montafon this time, she baked the Linzer torte herself and sent it over.
