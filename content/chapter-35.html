After spending a night at Hotel Bärgerhof, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park woke before dawn. The moon was still hanging above the peaks of Montafon, a half moon gone faint in the first light. Through the large window of their room, the mountain facing them, a ridge of the Alps, seemed close at hand. These were the mountains of the Alpine Montafon valley. Author Jeong, who had risen first, walked to the window and suddenly cried out.

“Sweetheart, look at that mountain!”

“What is it?”

“The painting on the folding fan by painter Kim Yangsu looks exactly like the mountain in front of us.”

The half moon floating above the bluish peaks washed with dawn, and the white stream running almost vertically down between the slopes to the right, looked astonishingly like the painting on the folding fan. Although Author Jeong had never told painter Kim Yangsu that he would be traveling to the Alpine Montafon and asked him to paint its scenery, the fact that the painter depicted Montafon's moon and mountains without knowing Montafon at all could only be explained as his inspiration. Besides, was not the title of the novel Author Jeong had already begun serializing Montafon Moonlight? It could only have been an intersection wrought in the unconscious of Author Jeong, Mr. Willi, and painter Kim Yangsu.

Mrs. Park slipped the folding fan into her handbag. Author Jeong was curious to see how Mr. Willi would react upon receiving the folding fan as a gift. Chairwoman Kwon, who had already been given a folding fan painted with azaleas in full bloom, might be less surprised. At the appointed hour of nine, Chairwoman Kwon also came down to the hotel lobby. Author Jeong, Mrs. Park, and Chairwoman Kwon stepped outside the hotel, where red geraniums were blooming vividly, and waited for Mr. Willi. To the left of the road stood a white church with a small bell tower. To reach Mr. Willi's house one had to go down the road to the right. Beyond the cliff on the mountainside lay the village of St. Anton, where he lived. After Mr. Willi and Chairwoman Kwon exchanged morning greetings, Mr. Willi said,

“I’m not sure how the weather will turn out. Montafon weather is very fickle.”

“We’ll be doing nothing but interviews at your house all day, Mr. Willi, so it should be fine.”

Before long, the party drove down to the village of St. Anton in Mr. Willi's car. About ten minutes later they arrived at his house, with its little garden. They immediately climbed the exterior wooden staircase to the second floor. To Author Jeong, the interior felt more familiar than it had on his 2015 visit.

The piano on the left side of the hallway leading to the living room stood where it had always been. High on the wall opposite the entrance, a new addition caught his eye: a mungnando (墨蘭圖), an ink orchid painting, a type of Korean painting. Its Chinese character inscription, Dahyang malli (茶香萬里), meant that the fragrance of tea travels ten thousand li.

On the left side of the living room stood a long cabinet that looked as though it had been made by Mr. Willi's father, who had also been a carpenter, and on top of it the first thing to catch the eye was the ceramic owl Mrs. Park had given him in 2015. On the wall above the cabinet, the large, dreamlike, surrealist oil painting they had seen before still seemed to welcome Author Jeong and Mrs. Park. On the wall nearby to the right, the figure of Jesus nailed to the cross, carved by Mr. Willi's grandfather, remained in place as well. In the living room and dining room hung two Korean paintings they had not seen before. One was a Korean painting depicting three pine trees, and the other was a miindo (美人圖), a portrait of a beauty in hanbok holding a fan. On the wall right beside the dining-room door there was another figure of Jesus, this one by Mr. Willi's father, and in the dining room there was also a painting simply propped on the floor, as if there were nowhere to hang it. It showed musicians playing their instruments while a dancing boy (mudong) performed, reminiscent of Kim Hongdo's genre paintings. Not only that, in a corner of the dining room there was also a doll of a woman in hanbok playing the geomungo.

Looking at this all, it was clear that Mr. Willi had a great fondness for things Korean. Although he had many devoted fans in Japan, the paintings and dolls in his own house had all, without exception, been brought from Korea. As he himself had confessed, there was no telling whether he really had been Korean in a past life. Perhaps because of the Korean paintings, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park felt at ease. Before beginning the interview, Author Jeong presented Mr. Willi with the folding fan he had brought. He said,

“This is a folding fan I received from painter Kim Yangsu in Korea. But the picture on it is remarkably like the mountains of Montafon. It’s astonishing.”

“Ah, I see. I would like to know the painter's background in detail.”

“He used to teach at a university, but now he has gone down to his hometown of Jindo as a full-time painter who does nothing but paint. I will have Chairwoman Kwon write out his biography for you separately.”

The party sat at the round table in the living room and spent a while taking in the view beyond the large window. The village beyond the window was impeccably clean. Mr. Willi explained that because Europeans visited the Montafon valley, even at around a thousand meters above sea level, there were many hotels and guesthouses, all doing brisk business year-round.

Rain clouds were already hanging on the sharp peaks. The village on the slope across from them kept appearing and disappearing beneath avalanche-like banks of mist. It was as though the mountain spirit of Montafon were performing magic for Author Jeong and Mrs. Park. Suddenly Chairwoman Kwon turned to Author Jeong and apologized.

“There is something I must apologize to you for, Mr. Jeong.”

“What is it?”

“It was when I went to Korea in 2017 and visited your home in Hwasun. You gave me a book to pass on to Mr. Willi and signed it for him.”

“Did I? What book was it?”

“It was a very lovely Seon dongwha (禪童話), or Seon fairy tale, The Fool Novice. But when I returned to Austria, I left it in my room and completely forgot about it. I happened to come across it this time and brought it along.”

Before handing The Fool Novice to Mr. Willi, Chairwoman Kwon opened the book to the page Author Jeong had signed and showed it to him. Sure enough, Author Jeong's inscription read as follows.

To Mr. Willi,

A single raindrop is the universe,

and the universe is a single raindrop.

August 4, 2017

Sincerely, Jeong Chanju

When Chairwoman Kwon translated Author Jeong’s inscription into German for Mr. Willi, he smiled and said,

“These are true words. I agree. My own life in music began, too, with the sound of a single raindrop I heard on the Inn River at Innsbruck.”

Mrs. Park said,

“So, Chairwoman Kwon didn’t give Mr. Willi this book even when we came to Vienna in 2018.”

“No, it is even better that Chairwoman Kwon brought it now. I wrote, ‘A single raindrop is the universe,’ as my own interpretation of the sound of raindrops Mr. Willi heard in his despair at seventeen. If there is time today, even though I have heard it many times, I would like to hear that story once again.”

At Mrs. Park’s words, Author Jeong answered that things had turned out for the better. Then Chairwoman Kwon, looking apologetic, said,

“As a gesture of apology, I will translate The Fool Novice into German and read it aloud right here.”

Chairwoman Kwon began to read The Fool Novice slowly and distinctly. Mr. Willi listened, resting his chin on one hand.

“The temple was already more than five hundred years old. The pillars of the Dharma Hall were deeply furrowed with wrinkles, and the places where knots had once been had rotted through, leaving holes like a moktak (wooden bell). In spring, chickadees sometimes raised their young in those holes. There were times when a monk plugged the holes up, saying the hall was too noisy, but thanks to the child, the chickadee mothers would always return to raise their chicks. On bright moonlit nights, the child secretly opened the holes again behind the monks' backs.

The room where the monks studied was just as worn. Wild grasses sprouted and bloomed between the broken roof tiles. Yellow dandelions flowered about and sometimes purple thistles bloomed. Evening primroses opened their petals in the daytime and yawned, only to be caught by the child.

There were many mice in the ceiling of the monks’ old room as well. Their squeaking could be heard at all hours.

“Squeak, squeak squeeeak squeak.”

At mealtimes the mice grew even noisier, because the smell of sannamul (wild mountain greens), ssukguk (mugwort soup), and songi (pine mushrooms) set out in the monks' alms bowls spread through the whole ceiling. The monks never fed the mice, not ever. They said the act of stealing food in secret was the mice's karma (eob, 業). They believed that only by working off that “karma of stealing food” themselves, as if doing homework, could the mice be reborn in bodies better than a mouse's. So, sorry as they were, they said they could not help them.

“They must be hungry. Wouldn’t it be wonderful if, for just one day, we could set a table for the mice.”

It was precisely at this passage that light came into Mr. Willi's eyes. After Chairwoman Kwon finished translating and reading the rest of The Fool Novice into German, Author Jeong offered a remark.

“The child's heart is precisely the Buddha's heart. I believe that your heart, Mr. Willi, is the very heart of the foolish novice in the fairy tale.”

Mrs. Park said with a laugh,

“You are a foolish grown-up, Mr. Willi, a foolish composer. My husband is a fool too. He knows nothing but writing. Even now, he doesn’t know how to use a bank card.”

“The title of the storybook gives me chills. The Fool Novice… Mr. Willi, you seem almost like a foolish novice who insists on silence. Your heart is like that of an innocent child.”

“That is right. I wrote the fairy tale because I wanted to say how important silence is. In the story, the child's silence may be the same thing as Mr. Willi's absolute stillness.”

At that moment Chairwoman Kwon brought refreshments out from the dining room.

“This is a cake our Frida made. It is called a Linzertorte. Linz is the city we passed through yesterday. According to the historical record, the Linzertorte is said to be the oldest cake in the world.”

Frida was Mr. Willi's second partner, and Author Jeong and Mrs. Park had also met her in 2015. They had eaten together at a Thai restaurant; she was a woman with one son, and because of work commitments she had not been able to join them for the Montafon folk play. Chairwoman Kwon said that when Frida heard Author Jeong and Mrs. Park were coming to Montafon this time, she had baked the Linzertorte herself and sent it over.
