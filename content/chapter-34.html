The train that left Vienna Central Station at 10:30 raced without pause all the way to Salzburg Station. Broad meadows and pastures streamed endlessly past the window. In 2015, Dr. Werner had driven them to Montafon in his own car, but now that he had passed away, Author Jeong and his wife, along with Chairwoman Kwon, had no choice but to travel by train. At times a river would appear close by, then vanish again. The mountains still kept their distance. Chairwoman Kwon said,

“If the meadows are well maintained, the government provides subsidies. That is why people care for them as carefully as if they were tending their own gardens.”

“In Korea, farmers also receive a direct payment paid out by the state. But it seems they do not extend such payments to the meadows on the mountain slopes.”

It seemed to be a case of mutual flourishing between state and individual: private people raised their household income by tending the mountainside meadows well, while the state could show tourists an Austria of peaceful nature. Author Jeong had assumed the pastoral landscape simply came into being on its own, and he nodded at the realization that it was in fact a joint work of the state and the individual.

It was long after they had passed Salzburg Station. Rock mountains shaped like pyramids had drawn near before they knew it. As the mountains came closer, the meadows grew narrower. The grasslands, shrinking the farther the train ran, had in places climbed up onto the gently sloping skirts of the mountains. After the train emerged from two or three long tunnels, the city of Innsbruck came into view, home to the theological college from which Mr. Willi had graduated in his youth and the hospital where he had been admitted as a boy with an incurable illness.

“We still have three or four more stops before Bludenz Station.”

“So, there is no train that goes all the way to Montafon.”

“Montafon is a very remote place even within Vorarlberg. Mr. Willi said he would drive out to Bludenz Station to meet us.”

“He must be busy, and yet we are putting him to so much trouble.”

“When I told him we were coming by train, he was the one who first offered to come and pick us up in his car.”

“Hahaha. If he has been waiting for us so eagerly, that is understandable enough.”

The train’s final stop was Bregenz, the capital of Vorarlberg. When there were about two stations left, the party took their suitcases down from the rack. Then they stepped out into the passageway between the cars. None of the other passengers had come out yet. Chairwoman Kwon said with a smile,

“It is a Korean trait. When you see people hurrying and rushing, most of them are Korean. Look. Not a single Austrian has come out yet.”

“That is true. We are the only ones out here.”

A train crew member announced the upcoming station. Mrs. Park said,

“I’m not sure this is our stop.”

Only when the upcoming station came into sight did three or four Austrian passengers come out into the passageway. The party stood on tiptoe in the passageway to make way for them.

“If Dr. Werner were still alive, you could have gone comfortably by car. I am sorry.”

“Not at all. If I remember correctly, Dr. Werner drove for eight hours that time. But today the train takes only six, so in a way it seems to save time.”

“If you want to tour the Alps, a car is certainly better, but there is pleasure in traveling by train, too.”

The train that had left Vienna Central Station at 10:30 was due to arrive at Bludenz Station at 4:30 in the afternoon. That being so, in about another thirty minutes the train would be pulling into Bludenz Station.

“You must feel something special, returning to Montafon after ten years.”

“I met Mr. Willi once in Montafon in 2015 and twice in Vienna in 2018, so this is the fourth time, yet my heart is fluttering as though I were on my way to meet a friend.”

“And you too, Mrs. Park?”

“I want to hear Mr. Willi talk about music again.”

Mrs. Park, an ardent lover of classical music, spoke as she recalled the shock she had felt in 2015 when she heard Mr. Willi’s music at his two-story home. He had played them his own music recorded on CD, and she had been unable to tear herself away from it for the whole day, as though seized by a hwadu.

At last, when they got off at Bludenz Station, silver-haired Mr. Willi came toward them smiling. The station building was small and unadorned, befitting a country station. He gave Chairwoman Kwon a light embrace. He warmly shook hands with Author Jeong and exchanged an affectionate glance with Mrs. Park. Instead of going into the station building, the party followed him straight to the parking lot and got into his car. Chairwoman Kwon and Mr. Willi exchanged the next few hours’ plans in German and then Chairwoman Kwon explained them to Author Jeong and his wife.

“Mr. Willi says that first we should go to your hotel, unpack, and then come down into the village for a meal.”

“It is not even six yet, so that sounds good.”

“I booked the hotel online. It is higher up than Mr. Willi’s house, and from there you can see the village and the mountain opposite all at once.”

For Author Jeong and his wife, the 2025 journey was a special one. Chairwoman Kwon was covering their round-trip airfare between Seoul and Vienna, along with all of their lodging. To that same degree, Chairwoman Kwon earnestly hoped that Author Jeong would write a work taking Mr. Willi’s life in music as its subject. Even so, it was not a binding obligation. The timing of the writing, its structure, and its content were all to be left entirely to Author Jeong’s own wishes. The method he had in mind was simple. Rather than displaying his own imagination, he meant to write down just what he had experienced, including the conversations he had exchanged with Mr. Willi. It would not be wrong to call it a kind of autobiographical novel.

Before long, the car passed through the village of St. Anton, where Mr. Willi lived, and stopped at the crest of the mountain road. The hotel where Chairwoman Kwon and Author Jeong and his wife were staying stood on high ground at about 1000m above sea level. Just beyond the parking lot in front of the hotel was a sheer, vertiginous cliff. There was no telling how skilled Mr. Willi was at driving, but watching him back into the space was enough to give one a sense of danger.

The lodging Chairwoman Kwon had reserved was Hotel Bergerhof. Mr. Willi waited in the hotel lobby while Chairwoman Kwon and Author Jeong and his wife received their room keys from the clerk at the counter and climbed the stairs to the second floor. Author Jeong and his wife had a room with a fine view. The massive peaks of the mountain opposite faced them directly. No sooner had Author Jeong and his wife set their suitcases beside the bed than they went back down to the hotel lobby, where Mr. Willi was waiting. Chairwoman Kwon came down from her own room after them.

The sun had not yet set. The remaining daylight, the final glow of the day, was gentle rather than strong. It fell impartially on the well-paved slope, the neat roads of the village, and the stream flowing through the valley, as well.

After parking his car in a lot beside the stream, Mr. Willi showed them the notable places in the village. In particular, in front of the Posthotel Taube, he said the owner was a friend and suggested they take a commemorative photograph. The dove design above the hotel’s name was striking, too.

“Since Hemingway stayed and wrote here, I think this hotel will remain in your memory, Author Jeong.”

“Hemingway came all the way here?”

“I heard it from a friend who knows the history of this village well.”

Author Jeong could not know at once for what reason Hemingway had come all the way to Montafon, but he found it more than a little intriguing. In high school he had devoured Hemingway’s novels The Old Man and the Sea, A Farewell to Arms, and For Whom the Bell Tolls, and had been captivated by the clarity of his short sentences and his descriptions of action. Perhaps for that reason, there were acquaintances who said that the shadow of Hemingway flickered through works such as Gwangju Arirang, Author Jeong’s novel taking the May 18 Gwangju People’s Uprising as its subject.

Then Mr. Willi led the party to a building beside the stream.

“This is the house where I took clarinet lessons when I was seven. It had a grand piano, something rare in Montafon. But the old teacher made me miserable. While teaching me how to play the clarinet, whenever I made a mistake he would punish me at every turn, pulling my ears and the like. It made me want to give up music. Fortunately, my father had taught himself to play various instruments, and I was happy I could take music lessons from him.”

Mr. Willi said that his father, who had grown up a war orphan during the Second World War, could not only play many instruments but also compose, and was so deft with his hands that he could even repair Montafon’s traditional wardrobes. Then the party followed Mr. Willi as far as the bridge over the stream.

“Watching the forceful waters of this stream, I conceived the second movement of Jeong (情).”

The stream, rising in the foothills of the Alps, was jade-colored, and though its volume was not great, it flowed with a lively brightness. To say that he conceived the second movement of Jeong while looking at this water meant, if one were to understand it through the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures, that like a cowherd discovering the ox’s footprints, Mr. Willi had at last glimpsed the first clue to the music he had been seeking.

“Thinking of it now, I feel that your music, Mr. Willi, is a gift given by your father and by the natural world of the Alpine Montafon.”

“My mother too was a war orphan, so she had no chance to study and knew little about music. But her interest cannot be left out either. After attending one of my premieres, she would say, ‘This piece will be performed many times,’ or, ‘This piece must wait a little longer for its time,’ and she was almost always right.”

The Italian restaurant Mr. Willi had reserved was about a ten-minute walk from the bridge over the stream. The streets were quiet, but inside the dim restaurant it was crowded with customers. The party followed the waiter to a table at the back of the restaurant and sat down. Author Jeong and his wife had wanted to order the spaghetti alle vongole they had often eaten while traveling in Italy, but it was not on the menu. With no other choice, Chairwoman Kwon ordered them a salty kind of spaghetti instead. While they ate, Mr. Willi spoke his own views on music, and Author Jeong listened closely.

“Music exists at the boundary between what is seen and unseen. A musician is one who makes that visible.”

He also spoke of the experience of fighting illness in his youth that had brought him to awakening.

“When I was seventeen and suffering from an incurable disease, I was treated so often that there was hardly any place left in my arms and legs for a needle. Then one day, all at once, I began to watch calmly the self that had left behind the pain of the body.”

As if to console him, Author Jeong said,

“To look objectively at yourself after leaving behind such extreme bodily pain, might that not mean that, having become no-self (無我, mua), you became one with the world?”

Chairwoman Kwon laughed and said it was difficult to interpret. But Mr. Willi and Author Jeong communicated without any real difficulty. Author Jeong expressed himself with only a few English words he knew, and Mr. Willi understood him at once.

Just then some Roma musicians came in. One played the violin, and another sang “Bella Ciao” in lively fashion. Mr. Willi then handed five euros to the musician holding his hat upside down.

“Here in Montafon, the Italian border is very close. So Roma musicians sometimes make their way all the way over here.”

The musicians went from table to table, playing the violin, singing, and collecting tips. To the strains of their violin, the Montafon night quietly deepened.
