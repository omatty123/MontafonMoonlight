The Linz cake Frida had sent was cut by Chairwoman Kwon and shared out among Mr. Willi, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park. She also brewed coffee in the kitchen where the sink was and poured some into each person's cup. Mr. Willi, who ate only one meal a day, said that he did not need lunch himself but offered to cook for Author Jeong and Mrs. Park, but the couple felt the cake alone would serve as lunch. Author Jeong said,

“No, the cake alone will do for lunch.”

“I eat only once a day, but Korean food is so delicious I could eat it three times.”

“In that case, next time we come, we'll bring instant rice, kimchi, gim, and gochujang.”

In Mrs. Park's suitcase there were single-serving bowls of instant rice that could be heated quickly in the microwave, along with vacuum-packed spicy kimchi, savory gim, and hot gochujang in a tube. They had brought them from Korea to eat whenever they grew tired of European food. While they were eating cake, Chairwoman Kwon said,

“Now let's talk about something fun. Mr. Willi, what was the name of the girlfriend you had when you were in school in Montafon?”

“A girlfriend? From the time I was fifteen until I was seventeen, I would spend two months at a time in the hospital in Innsbruck and then come back to Montafon, so I wasn't in a position to have a girlfriend.”

“So it was a repeated cycle of two months in the hospital, then discharge, then home again.”

At Author Jeong's words, Mr. Willi said,

“From the age of fifteen, I thought, If this goes on, I'll end up on a road of death from which I will never come home again. It made me terribly sad. Still, I held onto a sliver of hope: I will overcome this illness. I will survive. In any case, I went back and forth between the hospital and home, and attended school only in form. Thanks to my homeroom teacher and my friends, I somehow managed to finish school. But because I was absent so often, I didn't have the chance to get close to my classmates one by one. Even so, my classmates wrote me letters to comfort me. Even now, when I read those letters, my chest tightens and tears come to my eyes.”

“Were they saying things like, ‘We'll pray to God that you recover,’ and so on?”

“Most of them said, ‘We feel terribly lonely without you. We miss you.’”

“In Korean terms it sounds like the equivalent of high school. What kind of help did your homeroom teacher and your friends give you?”

“In Austria, high school goes on until age eighteen, and the teachers and students treated me with tremendous understanding. One day my homeroom teacher said, ‘Graduation is not far off, so don't come to school; stay at home.’ The students helped me too. Montafon was the countryside, so the school was small and there were not many students. There were thirty-six students in our year in all, four girls and the rest boys. The ones who helped me were the girls. Because I couldn't come to school often, those four girls copied down what the teachers taught and gave it to me. I studied on my own from their notes. Thanks to them, I was able to keep up good grades without attending classes.”

Thanks to the kindness of those Montafon schoolgirls, Mr. Willi had been able to keep the grades he needed to go to university. Author Jeong asked another question.

“What did you do at home besides study? It must have been tedious at times.”

“I spent a great deal of time alone, both in the hospital and at home. So from a certain day onward, I began stopping by the bookshop in Montafon. It wasn't because there was any particular book I wanted to buy. I would buy and read whatever book happened to catch my eye. Without any knowledge of books, I simply bought and read the ones my hand went to without my even knowing it.

At one time, too, I didn't have a record player. Then one day I bought one, and after that I tried to collect as many records as I could. As I listened to and learned from all that recorded music, I wanted to hold fast to the belief that I would live a long, long time.”

Author Jeong thought that Mr. Willi's unconscious purchases at the bookshop, and his urge to collect many records after buying a record player, were both perhaps ways of trying to sustain a faith in life itself, the faith that “I will live a long time.” His story was shot through with the desperate wish to escape an incurable illness.

“There were happy moments too.”

“So this isn't a hospital story.”

“That's right.”

Mr. Willi finished the coffee remaining in his cup. Then he propped his chin on his hand and let his eyes drift into the air. It was his habit whenever he summoned up some distant corner of his past. At such moments, tears would gather at his eyes when the memory was sad, while a smile would play there when it was joyful. This time there was a smile not only in his eyes but at the corners of his mouth.

“When I was seventeen, I spent a month in London, England. There was a girl among the students named Ulli. Her full name was Ulrike, but Ulli was her nickname. All of a sudden Ulli asked me, ‘Hey, you know, I'm going to England for a month. Would you like to come too?’”

“She must have been one of the girls who was good at taking notes.”

“No, someone else was the good note-taker. Ha ha ha.”

Author Jeong said it as if he had guessed the answer, but Mr. Willi laughed and immediately waved his hand. Then he went on with the story.

“Ulli said that two of her relatives lived in London: she would stay with one, and I could stay with the other. We would live separately for a month, then meet at the airport on the last day, board the plane, and return to Montafon together.”

“So that is how you ended up going to England.”

However, on the promised last day, Mr. Willi did not get on the plane with Ulli. She never appeared at the airport. The next time Ulli turned up in Montafon was the day before the new term began. She had found herself a boyfriend and stayed in London until then before returning.

“Ulli's boyfriend drove a splendid car trimmed in wood. Every day he took Ulli around London in it, and in the evening he would bring her back to her relatives' house. In the end, Ulli later married that boyfriend, and I heard that she now lives in America. Ulli has a son, and his name is Amadeus.”

“Ha ha ha…”

The moment Mr. Willi mentioned the name of Ulli's son, not only Author Jeong and Mrs. Park but even Chairwoman Kwon burst out laughing. Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart is one of the greatest composers Austria boasts of. How could they not laugh, when of all names Ulli's son was called Amadeus? As though the stopper had come out, Mr. Willi went on in high spirits.

“I have a souvenir I brought back from London. I discovered it while walking down a certain street—it was a lane lined with record shops. With the little spending money I had left, I bought a symphony by Anton Bruckner. I spent every last coin on it, thinking that one day I would listen to it all. There is still a symphony from that purchase that I have never listened to. Ha ha ha.”

Since it was an incident from more than fifty years ago, it was something Mr. Willi could laugh about. Chairwoman Kwon and Author Jeong and Mrs. Park laughed along with him.

“Our house too is packed tight with composers' records. When I grow old, I suppose I'll have a vast amount of time. Then I'll have no choice but to listen to them all. Ha ha ha.”

Author Jeong thought this would remain in his memory as the day he had laughed most during his interviews with Mr. Willi. When they met in 2015 or 2018, Mr. Willi's musical world had been too profound, and the pain of his youth spent with an incurable illness had pressed on them too heavily for them to laugh out loud as they had today.

Again, as they went on talking, the story of Mr. Willi's youth returned, yet it did not cast Author Jeong and Mrs. Park into gloom. They had heard it several times since 2015, and so they had come to understand his unhappy adolescence with a certain objectivity. The story of how, by the Inn River in Innsbruck, he heard the sound of raindrops at the very moment thunder and lightning split the sky darkened with storm clouds, and how, even after hearing a despairing diagnosis at the Zurich hospital he had gone to with his father in hope, he nevertheless believed, “I will live”—these stories too they listened to calmly.

The interview with Mr. Willi ended at around three in the afternoon. After driving Chairwoman Kwon and Author Jeong and Mrs. Park back to the hotel, he said,

“I'll come back to the hotel at six this evening.”

Chairwoman Kwon and Author Jeong and Mrs. Park rested at the hotel. Even though it was already six in the evening, Mr. Willi was planning to guide them to the Posthotel Taube in the Montafon valley. Chairwoman Kwon explained that because Montafon had the phenomenon of white nights, it stayed light until nine p.m. As they rested, Author Jeong carefully noted down in his notebook the things he and Mr. Willi had said to each other.

When six o'clock came, Mr. Willi drove up to the hotel in his car. The party took the winding paved mountain road to the Posthotel Taube. When they arrived at the Taube, they went into the hotel café and ordered coffee and bread. There were only one or two other guests in the café. Chairwoman Kwon said,

“If it were ski season, it would be bustling.”

Just then Mr. Willi pointed to the open ground outside the building.

“The hotel owner is thinking of building a concert hall there and naming it after me.”

The hotel owner, too, was evidently a lover of music. Mr. Willi said he was the first person ever to propose building a concert hall in Montafon bearing his name. Yet Author Jeong could not help thinking it would be a long time in coming. Meaningful things, after all, are beset by that many twists and turns and are always slow to come to fruition.
