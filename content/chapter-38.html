As he treated himself at home, Mr. Willi thought of the middle-aged woman he had met on the train a few months earlier. The memo with the telephone number she had given him came to mind. Mr. Willi found the memo and decided to call, as though he were finally doing homework he had put off. No name or surname was written on the memo. There was only a telephone number. Calming his trembling heart, Mr. Willi dialed. The person who answered was a man.

“A woman gave me this telephone number and told me I could receive help,” he said. “I know nothing about the person who has answered. My name is Willi, and I live in the village of St. Anton in Montafon.”

“I am Pater von Lama. When can you come to me?”

“As soon as possible.”

“Then come tomorrow.”

With a name like Lama, Mr. Willi thought, he must be a Catholic priest. He received the priest’s Innsbruck address over the telephone. The next day, Mr. Willi took the train from Bludenz station to Innsbruck. As he gazed through the train window at the distant mountains, certain houses appeared before him. There were benches, and he saw a house with a great deal of furniture. In one room of that house, a man was sitting in a chair before a large desk. Such scenes appeared for about ten minutes, then vanished.

“I was simply looking through the train window at the mountains in the distance, not thinking anything special at all, and yet those scenes appeared to me.”

In any case, once he arrived in Innsbruck, Mr. Willi called a taxi and went to the address the man had given him. He lived in a monastery. When Mr. Willi entered through the monastery gate, he saw a series of different rooms. Then the room he had seen from the train caught his eye. He went toward it. In front of the door, he hesitated, wondering whether to knock. Just then, he heard an old man’s voice.

“Come in.”

The moment he entered, Mr. Willi was certain it was the room he had seen from the train. The man sitting in the chair was an old priest. The priest was over ninety, and because moving about was difficult for him, he seemed to spend his days only in that room. For a while, Mr. Willi sat respectfully on a small chair beside the door, his two hands folded. Then the old priest told him to stand. The priest rose from his chair and came toward him. His build was almost the same as Mr. Willi’s. The priest stretched his left hand over Mr. Willi’s head and placed his right hand low on Mr. Willi’s back. And then it happened. Mr. Willi fell into an unconscious state in which he did not know whether he was standing, lying down, or sitting. He only felt that his body was hot.

“Suddenly I existed alone inside a chamber of searing heat, though I did not know what sort of place it was. I felt I was certainly existing somewhere other than here. It was very bright there, and I was not alone.”

Then he heard a gentle voice, like an echo.

“There is work for us to do. Will you go back and do yours? I have many matters to resolve. I must finish my assignment.”

Only then did Mr. Willi return to reality. He saw the old priest, who told him to sit. The old priest then went outside, brought in a glass of water, and told Mr. Willi to drink it; then he repeated the same action twice more. As he did, the old priest said,

“God wills it, and your illness will be healed.”

Each time Mr. Willi drank, it was so difficult that he appealed to the old priest.

“I cannot possibly drink it.”

The first glass seemed like oily water, the second like vinegar water, and the third like salt water.

“You must drink it.”

Whenever Mr. Willi said he could not drink it, the priest made the sign of the cross and urged him firmly. After he finally drank all three glasses and returned home, Mr. Willi deliberately tried not to take the pills he had been taking. Since the doctor’s prescription had not cured him, he wanted to give even this a try. A month later, he called the priest again and went to the monastery. Again the priest gave him three glasses of holy water. Another month later, Mr. Willi visited the priest once more and, as before, drank three glasses of holy water.

That time, thinking that next time he would drink not oily water, vinegar water, or salt water but just plain water, Mr. Willi left the monastery to go home, then crossed to the small church opposite it. There he knelt and prayed.

“Lord, I offer myself to you.”

He prayed that way in the sense that he had been born again, that he had received a new life. Mr. Willi confessed this to Author Jeong, Mrs. Park, and Chairwoman Kwon.

“If the One who gave me new life has a purpose, then I must be a conduit. If there is work for me to do, then I live only within that task.”

For Mr. Willi, who had lived in suffering beyond imagination, it was a natural conclusion. Author Jeong had heard several times about Mr. Willi’s painful youth. Every day he had blood drawn, and depending on the results, he went back and forth between the hospital and school. When he went to the hospital, he invariably received radiation treatment.

In the six-person hospital room, patients suffering from incurable illnesses died after undergoing surgery. Even a very strong laborer died. After waking from anesthesia, the laborer shouted in agony and cried out to Mr. Willi, “Never have surgery.” When the doctor asked whether he would consent to surgery, Mr. Willi answered “No” in place of his father; that situation had been nothing short of hell.

Author Jeong felt that Mr. Willi was like a Catholic monk.

“Hearing you say that you live according to God’s purpose, I can understand why you went to seminary, Mr. Willi.”

“But I do not say God. I do not often use the word itself. Because that word is so abused and misused in this world, I would rather dare to say the Creator.”

“In any case, it seems the priest’s influence was great.”

“Yes. The raindrop on the Inn River gave me the experience of crossing from this visible world into another world that cannot be seen. And after meeting the priest, I recovered my health and have lived a new life within the task given to me, within the work that is mine to do.”

“Then we should say that after meeting the priest, you were cured of your illness.”

“Yes, that is right. From then on, I did not go to the hospital. Meanwhile, I began to hear the sounds of nature. When I walked and looked at a tree, I heard the tree’s sound; when I looked at an object, that was the beginning of a history in which I could hear the sounds of things.”

“Hearing that, the woman you met on the train seems like an angel. Could she have been an angel transformed into an ordinary woman? In Buddhism, Avalokitesvara (K. Gwanseeum, C. Guanshiyin) Bodhisattva appears in many forms in order to fulfill someone’s wish. It is also said that Avalokitesvara has eyes that see all things in the world and ears that hear its sounds.”

At Author Jeong’s words, Mr. Willi only smiled. He neither affirmed nor denied them.

After the afternoon interview, Author Jeong, Mrs. Park, and Chairwoman Kwon returned to the hotel and rested again as they had the day before until six in the evening. This was because they were going to visit the log house in the Montafon mountains where Mr. Willi had lived in seclusion for five or six years while composing. Around six, however, the weather suddenly turned cloudy. Author Jeong and Mrs. Park took umbrellas and went down to the hotel lobby. Mr. Willi was punctual. He was already waiting in the hotel parking lot before 6:10. Prior to setting out, he said,

“I would like to show you the place where I composed. The log house we are visiting today is the villa of a wealthy family in Montafon. My father once made furniture for that house, so they are close to us through that inyeon (karmic connection). When I asked, they gladly lent it to me for several years.”

“What pieces did you compose at that villa?”

“I composed the opera Brother of Sleep and Encounter there.”

“Ah, in Korea it is known as Sleeping Brother, but it is Brother of Sleep.”

“That’s right. Since it means that sleep and the protagonist Elias are brothers, Brother of Sleep is more accurate. And after spending years notating the sounds of nature that came from the area around the log house, I was commissioned to write a piece for the Vienna Philharmonic Orchestra’s 150th anniversary and composed Encounter.”

As the car entered the mountains, rain began to fall in a steady drizzle. The log house where Mr. Willi had stayed to compose for five or six years was unexpectedly close. It seemed enclosed within the mountains. White veil-like rain clouds had descended all the way to the slopes and covered the spruce forest, but fortunately the log house stood clearly revealed. There were two buildings, divided by a single spruce tree. The upper building was a shed where firewood had been stacked neatly, and the lower building was a three-room residential log house. Mr. Willi opened his umbrella and went first toward the residential log house, then opened the wooden door. The party looked once around the inside of the log house, where there was a fireplace and a bed, and came back out. As Author Jeong was studying the stuffed rooster on the windowsill beside the entrance, Mr. Willi said,

“It is the mascot used during the Montafon festival. A rooster that survives the cold climate of the Alps symbolizes tenacious life-force.”

Even though it was eight in the evening, the slopes of the Alps around Montafon were bright with the white-night phenomenon. Mr. Willi drove the party deeper into the mountains. When they arrived at a gorge and got out of the car, rain clouds wrapped around them. The cold droplets felt as if they might freeze Author Jeong and Mrs. Park at any moment. Yet Mr. Willi, adapted to the Alpine climate, was untroubled in only a thin jumper and jeans, like the strong Montafon rooster they had just seen.

“This is the very place that gave me inspiration when I composed the opera Brother of Sleep. On that rock, young Elias came down from heaven and cried out, ‘What is my other half?’”

Mr. Willi stamped his foot vividly as he explained. Author Jeong and Mrs. Park felt their bodies slowly freezing, but because Mr. Willi was explaining with such sincerity, they could not bring themselves to say they wanted to get back into the car.

“The half Elias cried out for—is it not an invisible soul?”

“It is love.”

“Then the theme of Brother of Sleep is love.”

“Yes.”

Saying this was the last Alpine mountain village, Mr. Willi put the party back into the car and guided them all the way to the dead end of the road. There were a few houses there where cattle were raised. Mr. Willi explained that the mountain villagers made their living by producing cheese from the milk of cows they grazed. But Author Jeong and Mrs. Park had dressed lightly, prepared only for Vienna’s weather, and could think only of descending quickly. Seeing the two of them shivering, Chairwoman Kwon urged them to stay inside the car. Author Jeong, unlike Mr. Willi, grew anxious, worried that if he caught a cold the next stage of the journey might be affected.
