After parting with Mr. Willi at the entrance of the Wiener Konzerthaus, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park did not immediately board a flight back to Seoul. Chairwoman Kwon had suggested they visit Prague in the Czech Republic and Budapest in Hungary, both only two or three hours away by car. She arranged for them to use an IMK company car and dispatched a driver to serve as their guide.

After returning to Korea, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park could not forget Chairwoman Kwon's generosity. The impression Prague left on Author Jeong, in particular, would not easily fade. The road to Prague had felt like a journey to meet Kafka himself. During his high school years, when Author Jeong had been steeped in existentialist philosophy, the two writers who moved him most profoundly were Kafka and Camus. Gregor, the protagonist of Kafka's novella The Metamorphosis, and Meursault, the protagonist of Camus's novel The Stranger, had always struck him as something like half-brothers. That impression began with the very first sentence of each work.

'One morning, Gregor awoke from troubled dreams to find that, lying in his bed, he had been transformed into a hideous insect.' — Opening of The Metamorphosis

'Today, Mother died. Or perhaps it was yesterday; I cannot be sure. I received a telegram from the nursing home.' — Opening of The Stranger

Gregor's transformation into a hideous insect was a severing from the world, and the death of Meursault's mother felt like another such severing. For Gregor and Meursault, beings trapped in reality, these were absurd situations they had no choice but to accept.

In any case, the Kafka Museum near the banks of the Vltava was strange and uncanny. It was not a welcoming space designed for visitors' comfort. It was profoundly uncomfortable. One even felt a sense of the grotesque. The entire museum paid homage to Kafka's novel The Castle. In the museum courtyard stood bronze statues of two enigmatic men from the novel, along with a chair designed in the shape of the letter K. K was the protagonist of The Castle. The Castle itself was both a symbol of power that dominates human beings and a space representing a God that mortals can never reach.

The Castle was surely a novelistic device embodying Kafka's father, who wielded an authority akin to God's. In reality, Kafka's father was indeed an absolute figure, godlike in the young Kafka's eyes. A self-made man, the elder Kafka had ceaselessly berated his son: "I achieved all this despite the harshest conditions, yet you, who lack for nothing — why can you accomplish so little?" The Castle begins as follows.

'One night, the protagonist K arrives at a snow-covered village. K, a land surveyor, claims he has come to work at the Castle near the village, but the villagers do not believe him. The next morning, K sets out alone toward the Castle. Yet when he asks people for directions, they only smirk. K searches for the Castle with a growing sense of unease, but his efforts come to nothing. No matter how he searches, the Castle cannot be found. When he returns to the inn, two assistants are waiting for him. But they are not the assistants K had been working with. They are strangers of uncertain origin. Amid this eerie atmosphere and these encounters with unknown assistants, K tries again and again to reach the Castle, but every attempt ends in failure. The novel The Castle concludes with the proposition that one's purpose can never be fulfilled. What, in the end, is K's Castle? A God that humans have invented? A place that promises salvation? The novel reveals the absurdity of a world in which such things can never be reached, and so it ends.'

Stepping inside the Kafka Museum, Author Jeong felt a chill. It was as though he himself had become K. The museum felt like the Castle that K had tried so desperately to enter. Unlike other museums, its interior was dark as a cave. It had been deliberately designed to evoke the ambiguous, melancholy atmosphere of Kafka's own life. There was not a single window; all bright light had been shut out. The displayed photographs and documents were barely visible, illuminated only by small spotlights.

The next day, Budapest in Hungary also left a powerful impression on the couple. Mrs. Park was deeply moved at the Liszt Museum, while Author Jeong found himself stirred at the Attila József Literary Museum. For Mrs. Park, a lover of classical music, this was only natural. Attila was the poet whom UNESCO had honored in 2005 by declaring it the Year of Attila József, and his reputation was such that even in Korea his poetry collection The Seventh Person had been translated — Author Jeong himself had read it.

Budapest was a city formed in 1873 by the merger of Buda, on the right bank of the Danube, and Pest, on the left. Its population was roughly comparable to that of Daejeon in Korea, and its ancestors were the Huns and the Magyars, its history stretching back into deep antiquity. The IMK driver managed with some difficulty to park the car in a vacant lot on a back street. Budapest was lined with stately old buildings, yet there was something drab about the city. The shadow of socialism still seemed to hang over it. Women dressed in camouflage-patterned clothing reminiscent of military uniforms could be spotted here and there.

A quick search on their smartphones brought up the Liszt Museum on Sugár Street. The building that now housed the museum had once been the music academy where Liszt taught his students. Upon entering the two-story building, the first thing that caught their eye was a painting on the wall inscribed "DORÉ 1866." It was a depiction of Dante in Hell, painted by the artist Gustave Doré, who had drawn inspiration from Liszt's Dante Sonata and presented the work to the composer as a gift. Given that Liszt had not only read Dante's Divine Comedy but also Goethe's Faust, from which he composed his Faust Symphony, he was without doubt a composer who read literature with great devotion. In one room, a remarkably small instrument known as a "harmonica piano," which he had used when teaching students, was also on display. Near the exhibition hall, a notice on the door of a small concert hall announced an upcoming performance. Mrs. Park paused before the notice. She seemed unable to hide her regret at visiting the Liszt Museum without having the chance to hear any music.

The second place the party visited was the Attila József Literary Museum. Attila József (1905–1937), the poet most beloved by the Hungarian people. A worker-poet who went from pig-herding boy to glass factory laborer. Attila's father left for America when the boy was three, and the young Attila was placed with foster parents. His mother, a laundry worker, suffered from cancer and eventually died. Precocious and troubled, Attila attempted suicide at the age of nine. He drifted through a succession of occupations: day laborer, newspaper vendor, newspaper delivery boy, dockworker, ship's assistant, cornfield watchman, poet, translator. In the end, he threw himself before a freight train, surrendering his life at the age of thirty-two. An autobiographical statement he wrote in 1937 when applying for a position at a company reveals that the seeds of poetry had already been germinating.

'In the third-grade reader, I read the story of "Attila," king of the Huns, and began to lose myself in books. Because my name was also Attila, the story fascinated me all the more. I had heard that no Christian bore the name Attila, so the tale of King Attila astonished me. It was from that moment that I began to question my own existence.'

They had to ask passersby repeatedly before they finally found the Attila József Literary Museum. After ringing the doorbell and stating their purpose, someone opened the door. When Author Jeong told a young curator that he had come from Korea, the curator smiled and produced two business cards. They belonged to Kim Jaehwan, Director of the Korean Cultural Center in Hungary, and Professor Kim Eunggyo of Sookmyung Women's University. The museum had opened three years earlier, and Author Jeong, the curator said, was only the third Korean visitor. There was no charge for admission.

On the ground floor, photographs of Attila filled with fury, handwritten letters, and various small artifacts were on display. The curator mentioned that the statue of Attila stood on the bank of the Danube. The party drove up and down the riverside road two or three times before they finally spotted it. Attila sat hunched forward, gazing down at the blue Danube. He had removed his suit jacket and held a hat in one hand, his expression vacant, as though all hope had left him. Perhaps he was not looking at the Danube at all but composing poetry from the depths of despair. That "vacant expression" lingered in Author Jeong's mind like a hwadu, refusing to fade. Strangely, powerfully, it took hold of him.

After staying in Seoul for a time, Author Jeong arrived at his mountain retreat, Ibuljae, in Hwasun. Five days later, the afterimages and memories of the Czech and Hungarian journey finally released their hold on him. Only then did Author Jeong begin, in the early evening, to write a brief account of the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures he had promised Mr. Willi. The manuscript was finished surprisingly quickly, in just one hour. What Author Jeong wrote was as follows.

The Ten Ox-Herding Pictures — Ten stages in which the seeker searches for the ox (truth)

1. Setting out to seek the ox (The seeker enters the mountains in search of the ox, wandering from peak to peak.)

2. Discovering the footprints (Wandering through the mountains, the seeker at last discovers the ox's footprints.)

3. Seeing the ox (In the mountains, which are like his own mind, the seeker sees the ox.)

4. Catching the ox (The seeker seizes the halter of the ox as it tries to flee.)

5. Taming the ox (The seeker works to befriend the ox as one would a companion.)

6. Riding the ox home (The seeker rides the now-tamed ox back to the place from which they first departed.)

7. The ox is forgotten, the self remains (The seeker, content that seeker and ox have become one, rests in happiness.)

8. Both ox and self are forgotten (The seeker forgets even the thought that seeker and ox have become one.)

9. Returning to the source (The seeker arrives at the very place from which they set out to seek the ox.)

10. Returning to daily life in freedom (The seeker, now free, lives in peace amid the everyday world.)

P.S. Mr. Willi, your composition Jeong closely parallels the stages of the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures. In your case, the ox of the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures would be music. And the final tenth movement of Jeong, which pursues absolute freedom and absolute peace, corresponds to the final tenth stage of the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures.

Only after emailing the manuscript to Chairwoman Kwon did Author Jeong pour himself a cup of black tea. At last, he savored its deep fragrance. The Ten Ox-Herding Pictures manuscript felt like a period placed at the end of his Austrian journey. He could not know when he might travel to Austria again, but he knew he would miss it from time to time. Just then, Author Jeong heard the cry of a scops owl from the mountain behind Ibuljae, its call as raw as though the bird were coughing up blood. Mr. Willi, back in Montafon, was perhaps listening to the song of a nightingale.
