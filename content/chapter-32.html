After parting with Mr. Willi at the entrance to the Vienna Concert House, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park did not immediately board a flight back to Seoul. This was because Chairwoman Kwon had urged them to travel on to Budapest in Hungary and Prague in the Czech Republic, both two or three hours away by car. Chairwoman Kwon had thoughtfully provided an IMK company car and sent one of the company's drivers to guide Author Jeong and Mrs. Park.

After returning to Korea, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park could not forget Chairwoman Kwon's kindness. For Author Jeong in particular, his impression of Prague in the Czech Republic did not fade easily. The road to Prague had felt as though it were the day he was to meet Kafka. During his high school years, when he had immersed himself in existentialist philosophy, the writers who moved Author Jeong most deeply were Kafka and Camus. Gregor, the protagonist of Kafka's short story The Metamorphosis, gave him the sense of being like a half-brother to Meursault, the protagonist of Camus's novel The Stranger. It was so from the very first sentences of the two works.

'One morning, Gregor awoke from troubled dreams to find that, lying in his bed, he had been transformed into a monstrous insect.' Opening sentence of The Metamorphosis

'Mother died today. Or maybe yesterday, I don't know. I received a telegram from the home.' Opening sentence of The Stranger

Gregor's transformation into a monstrous insect was also a rupture from the world, and the death of Meursault's mother likewise seemed a rupture from the world. For Gregor and Meursault, as beings existing in reality, these were absurd situations that they had no choice but to accept.

In any case, the Kafka Museum near the banks of the Vltava was bizarre and grotesque. It was not a hospitable space for visitors. It was exceedingly uncomfortable. It even gave one a sense of the uncanny. The entire museum was an homage to Kafka's novel The Castle. In the museum courtyard were installed bronze statues of the two enigmatic men who appear in the novel, along with a chair designed in the shape of the letter K. K was the protagonist of Kafka's novel The Castle. The Castle was at once a symbol that rules over human beings and a space symbolizing a God that human beings cannot attain.

The Castle must have been a novelistic device giving form to Kafka's father, who possessed an authority like that of God. In reality, Kafka's father was indeed an absolute figure, almost like a god to Kafka. It is said that his self-made father constantly subjected Kafka to reproach and humiliation, saying, "I accomplished this much even under difficult circumstances, so why is it that you, who lack for nothing, can do no better than that?" The Castle begins as follows.

'One night, the protagonist K arrives at a snow-covered village. K, a land surveyor, says that he has come to work at the Castle near the village, but the villagers cannot believe what he says. The next morning, K climbs toward the Castle alone. But even when he asks people the way, they merely respond with cynical smiles. Feeling something strange, K searches for the Castle, but all his efforts are in vain. No matter how hard he looks, he cannot find the Castle. When he returns to the inn, two assistants are there. But they are not the assistants who had been working with K. They are unsettling figures of unknown identity. Amid this strange atmosphere and these encounters with unfamiliar assistants, K repeatedly tries to reach the Castle, but in the end all comes to nothing. Leaving behind the proposition that one's aim can never be fulfilled, the novel The Castle comes to an end. What, then, is K's Castle? Is it a god imagined by human beings? Is it a place that saves human beings? But the novel ends by showing the absurdity of a world in which such things cannot be reached.'

As soon as he stepped into the Kafka Museum, it felt eerie. It was as though Author Jeong himself had become K. The museum felt like the Castle that K had tried to enter. Unlike other museums, the interior was dark like a cave. It had been deliberately staged to create an atmosphere as ambiguous and melancholic as Kafka's life. There was not a single window, in order to shut out bright light. The displayed photographs and materials were only barely visible under small lamps.

The next day, Budapest in Hungary, which they visited by car, also left a powerful impression on Author Jeong and Mrs. Park. Mrs. Park was especially moved at the Liszt Museum, and Author Jeong at the Attila József Literary Museum. From the standpoint of Mrs. Park, who loved classical music, this was only natural. Attila was a poet for whom UNESCO had designated 2005 as the Year of Attila József, and moreover he was well known enough in Korea that his poetry collection The Seventh Person had been translated and read by Author Jeong.

Budapest was the city formed in 1873 by the union of Buda on the right bank of the Danube and Pest on the left bank. Its population was about the same as that of Daejeon in Korea, and its original forebears were the Huns and the Magyars, so its history was very deep. The IMK employee managed with difficulty to park the car in an empty space off a side road. But although Budapest was lined with old-fashioned buildings, it gave off a gloomy impression. It seemed as though the shadow of socialism still hung over it. Women dressed in clothes resembling mottled military uniforms occasionally caught the eye.

A search on a smartphone immediately brought up the Liszt Museum on Sugár Street. The present Liszt Museum is said to have been the music academy where Liszt taught his students. Upon entering the two-story building, the first thing they saw was a picture hanging on the wall with the words "DORÉ 1866." It was a picture that the artist Doré had painted of Dante in hell after drawing inspiration from Liszt's Dante Sonata, and then presented to Liszt as a gift. Since Liszt is said to have read not only Dante's Divine Comedy but also Goethe's Faust and to have composed the Faust Symphony, he was surely a composer who read literary works with deep devotion. Also on display in one room was a very small instrument, the so-called "piano harmonica," which he had used when teaching students. A notice announcing a concert on the door of the small hall right next to the exhibition room caught their attention. Mrs. Park paused for a moment in front of the notice. She seemed deeply disappointed that she had come to the Liszt Museum but could not listen to any music.

The second place the party visited was the Attila József Literary Museum. Attila József (1905–1937) was the poet most beloved by the Hungarian people. He was a worker-poet who had gone from being a pig-herding boy to a glass factory laborer. Attila's father left for America when he was three years old, so the young Attila was adopted by foster parents, and his mother, who worked as a laundress, suffered from cancer and died. The precocious Attila even attempted suicide at the age of nine. After drifting through many occupations, including day laborer, newspaper seller, newspaper delivery boy, dockworker, assistant seaman, guard at a corn farm, poet, and translator, Attila eventually gave up his life at the age of thirty-two by throwing himself in front of a freight train. Looking at the autobiographical statement he wrote in 1937 when entering a company, one can be certain that the seeds of poetry were already germinating at that time.

'In a third-grade reader, I read the story of "Attila," king of the Huns, and began to immerse myself in reading. Since my own name was Attila, it interested me all the more. I had heard that there was no Christian name Attila, so the story of King Attila astonished me. From that time on, I began to question my own existence.'

They finally found the Attila József Literary Museum only after asking people for directions. After pressing the doorbell and stating their business, someone opened the door. When Author Jeong told a young curator that he had come from Korea, the curator smiled and held out two business cards. They were the cards of Kim Jaehwan, director of the Korean Cultural Center in Hungary, and Professor Kim Eunggyo of Sookmyung Women's University. He said that the museum had opened three years earlier and that Author Jeong was only the third Korean visitor, and he did not charge an admission fee.

On the first floor of the literary museum were displayed photographs of Attila filled with anger, handwritten letters, and other small materials. The curator told them that Attila's statue was on the bank of the Danube. The party drove up and down the riverside road two or three times in the car before finally finding the statue. Attila was sitting in a stooped posture, looking down at the blue Danube. He had taken off his suit jacket and was holding a hat in one hand, and his vacant expression made him seem as though he had lost all hope. Perhaps he was not looking at the Danube at all, but composing poetry in despair. Attila's "vacant expression," like a hwadu, did not easily disappear from Author Jeong's mind. That "vacant expression" gripped Author Jeong with a strange and powerful force.

After staying in Seoul and then arriving at Ibuljae, his mountain retreat in Hwasun, it was not until five days later that Author Jeong was freed from the afterimages and memories of his travels in the Czech Republic and Hungary. Only then did Author Jeong begin in the early evening to write a very brief account of the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures that he had promised Mr. Willi. The manuscript was finished surprisingly quickly, in just one hour. What Author Jeong wrote on the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures was as follows.

The Ten Ox-Herding Pictures: ten stages in which the oxherd seeks the ox (truth)

1. Setting out to search for the ox (The oxherd enters the mountains in search of the ox, wandering from one mountain to another.)

2. Seeing the ox's footprints (After wandering in the mountains, the oxherd at last sees the ox's footprints.)

3. Seeing the ox (In the mountains, which are like their own mind, the oxherd sees the ox.)

4. Catching the ox (The oxherd grabs hold of the halter of the ox as it tries to disappear.)

5. Tending the ox (The oxherd tries to become close to the ox, like a friend.)

6. Riding the ox back home (The oxherd rides the ox, now grown close, back to the place from which they originally departed.)

7. The ox is forgotten and only the self remains (The oxherd is content and happy, thinking that together with the ox they have become one.)

8. Both self and ox are forgotten (The oxherd forgets even the thought that they have become one with the ox.)

9. Returning to the original place (The oxherd arrives at the place from which they had set out in search of the ox.)

10. Returning to everyday life and enjoying freedom (The oxherd becomes a free person and lives in peace while enjoying everyday life.)

P.S. Mr. Willi's Jeong closely resembles the stages of the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures. In Mr. Willi's case, the ox in the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures would be music. And the final tenth movement of Jeong, which pursues absolute freedom and absolute peace, corresponds to the final tenth stage of the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures.

Only after emailing the manuscript to Chairwoman Kwon did Author Jeong pour himself a cup of black tea. At last, he savored its deep fragrance. The Ten Ox-Herding Pictures manuscript felt like a period placed at the end of his Austrian journey. He could not know when he might travel to Austria again, but he knew he would miss it from time to time. Just then, Author Jeong heard the cry of a scops owl from the mountain behind Ibuljae, its call as raw as if it were coughing up blood. Mr. Willi, back in Montafon, was perhaps listening to the song of a nightingale.
