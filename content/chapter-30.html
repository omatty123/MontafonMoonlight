Back in Vienna after their stay in Krems, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park spent several days resting at the annex on Beethovengasse belonging to Chairwoman Kwon. They shook off the fatigue of their Italian travels by visiting the Leopold Museum to see the exhibition commemorating the centennial of the deaths of Klimt and Schiele.

That afternoon, they were to meet composer Herbert Willi at the Wiener Konzerthaus. It was the same Mr. Willi whom Author Jeong had visited over four days and three nights beginning September 15, 2015, in the Montafon valley with its views of the Alpine snowfields. When Chairwoman Kwon mentioned that a Korean novelist who wrote from a Buddhist worldview existed, Mr. Willi's curiosity was piqued and he had insisted on meeting Author Jeong, prompting the visit.

Author Jeong still vividly remembered staying at the guesthouse beside Mr. Willi's two-story home and spending three days in long conversation with him. As if they were old friends of the soul, they compared Mr. Willi's philosophy of composition with Writer Jeong's literary outlook and spoke together at great depth. For that reason, Writer Jeong felt that he could say he knew Mr. Willi better than anyone else. If he were to introduce the man who had recently become his friend, it would be something like this.

Chairwoman Kwon reported that Mr. Willi had completed a ten-movement work on the theme of Korean jeong and was searching for a venue for its premiere. When he showed the score to the brilliant principal conductor of the LA Philharmonic, Gustavo Dudamel, Dudamel declared he had found the sound he had been looking for and asked for the opportunity to give it a premiere.

What was music as Mr. Willi understood it? In an interview with a reporter from the Korean daily H, he had stated:

"The most important thing in my life and my composing is 'absolute silence.' When I walk through the Alpine forests for days or months in search of that silence and stillness, at a certain moment music begins to rise from a deep place within me, from inside my body. In that moment, rational judgment and analysis disappear, and I shed the membrane of ego and reason that surrounds me. A new world opens, one connected to the infinite universe. That is when I compose."

After a light lunch at their lodgings, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park set out to meet Mr. Willi. The Wiener Konzerthaus was a ten-minute taxi ride away. Chairwoman Kwon was waiting at the entrance and led them to the office where Mr. Willi would receive them.

This reunion with Mr. Willi had been arranged through Chairwoman Kwon at Author Jeong's request. He wanted to revisit parts of Mr. Willi's philosophy of music, his life story, that had remained unclear from their conversations three years prior. They were sipping tea with Chairwoman Kwon in the small office when Mr. Willi walked in wearing a black short-sleeved shirt and jeans, grinning with childlike openness.

"It has been a few days since we arrived in Austria after traveling through Tuscany."

"When I couldn't find a breakthrough in my composing, I lived for about a year near Pisa and Florence. In any case, I drew tremendous energy from Tuscany. All music truly exists there."

"If I were to return to Italy, I would go straight to Tuscany. It was that wonderful. The ancient castles rising at the edge of vast wheat fields were especially striking. I felt as if the inspiration for a novel might surge up at any moment."

"The existence of primeval forests in Tuscany was a great fortune for me as a composer. They influenced the music I have composed recently."

After briefly exchanging recent news, they dove straight into conversation, with Author Jeong posing the questions and Mr. Willi answering.

"Your recovery from the incurable illness you suffered as a boy seems nothing short of a miracle."

"That's right. The incurable disease I had at seventeen or eighteen was healed by drinking the water an elderly priest at a monastery chapel gave me."

"I recall you made a similar confession three years ago."

"Looking back now, I believe it was not mere water but something possessing a divinity beyond itself."

At this point, Mr. Willi spoke carefully. He said he would not continue if Author Jeong found it hard to believe, but Author Jeong urged him on, leaning in to listen.

"So I prayed in the monastery and vowed to dedicate myself to the Lord. After that I enrolled in medical school, hoping to become a doctor. But I soon dropped out and entered seminary. Seminary life was not smooth, either. In my second year, I asked a priest during a lecture, 'How does wine become the blood of Christ during Mass?' My classmates mocked me, and I took a leave of absence. In the end, after many twists and turns, I graduated from seminary, studied composition at the Salzburg conservatory, and then devoted myself entirely to creative work from the age of twenty-five to forty."

As their conversation flowed, Author Jeong felt a deepening affinity, above all, with the fact that Mr. Willi was a monist in the Eastern mold. Herbert Willi, believer that the self, nature, and the universe were not separate but one. A vegetarian who ate only one meal a day, like an ascetic Seon master. More surprising still was his confession that he might have been Korean in a past life. When he added that he would gladly eat three meals a day if they were Korean food, his confession did not feel hollow. Roughly two hours had passed.

"I'm sorry, but my schedule won't allow us to talk any longer. I have another engagement."

But Author Jeong was not sorry, for they had agreed to meet again the next morning at the same place. On the way back, a sudden downpour forced them to pause only briefly before the statue of Maria Theresa, the empress who had secured the stability of the Habsburg dynasty. Returning to their lodgings, they changed clothes. Author Jeong put on a modern-style hanbok, and Mrs. Park changed into a traditional hanbok.

They had dressed in Korean finery to see the ballet Giselle at the State Opera House. Chairwoman Kwon had procured the tickets, so all they had to do was show up. It was an embarrassing confession, but Author Jeong had never attended a ballet in his life. He looked up Giselle in a ballet dictionary and found the following:

Composed in 1841. With a libretto by the nineteenth-century French Romantic poet Théophile Gautier, music by Adolphe Adam, and choreography by Jean Coralli and Jules Perrot. The work depicts the love between the peasant girl Giselle and the nobleman Albert, singing of the pain of unfulfilled love and the eternity of love that transcends even death. A masterpiece of Romantic ballet, it premiered at the Paris Opéra. However, the Giselle we see today is not the 1841 original but a new production choreographed by Marius Petipa in Russia in 1860.

They arrived early at the State Opera House, which alongside the Burgtheater had become one of Vienna's great artistic landmarks. Women in kimonos caught their eye. Picking up a program for Giselle, they discovered that the dancer performing the role of the count was Japanese. The women in kimonos appeared to be fans who had traveled from Japan to see him.

Audience members gave thumbs up at the sight of Author Jeong's and Mrs. Park's hanbok. The moment they stepped into the second-floor seats, the scale took their breath away. Every seat across all six levels was full, and it was only a weekday. A theater this grand and an audience this devoted, Author Jeong thought, were what it took for a nation to claim its place among cultural powers.

The ballet Giselle, with its fusion of music and dance, was not in the least tedious, and its story came through without need for words. At last, Author Jeong and Mrs. Park truly felt they had arrived in Vienna, city of the arts.

The next day. Author Jeong and Mrs. Park returned to the Wiener Konzerthaus where they had gone the previous afternoon, this time to meet Mr. Willi again. He had promised to explain the background of the ten movements he had spent more than a decade composing on the theme of Korean jeong. When they entered the office, the wall clock read 9:40. Author Jeong and his wife listened as Mr. Willi described in detail the compositional intent behind each movement, from the first to the tenth.

"I composed the process of searching for music in ten stages. The theme of Jeong is absolute peace and absolute freedom."

Author Jeong was reminded of the Buddhist Ten Ox-Herding Pictures, a series of ten images depicting the stages of seeking an ox, which represents truth. He sketched the ox-herding images on a notepad and showed them to Mr. Willi, who was fascinated and deeply sympathetic.

"The music I composed also flows from the first movement through the ninth in pursuit of absolute freedom and absolute peace, then in the tenth movement returns to the original silence."

"It is remarkably similar to the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures, which depict the stages of Buddhist practice in pursuit of awakening."

In the Ten Ox-Herding Pictures, one departs from the marketplace into the mountains in search of the ox of truth, and after finding it, returns to the marketplace in a state of jeongmyeol, silent peace, having forgotten even the ox. The structural arc was strikingly similar.

"I wish for my composition Jeong to have its premiere at Panmunjom on the Korean Peninsula, where true peace is needed most."

"When I return to Korea, I will do my best to make that happen."

Author Jeong, who was well acquainted with the Korean prime minister, resolved inwardly that upon his return he would watch for an opportunity and do what he could to arrange it.
